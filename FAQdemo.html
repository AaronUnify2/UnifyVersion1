  • Do I need approval to build on my rural block?
    ⌄

    Almost certainly yes. A new house, shed, or carport needs building approval, and depending on the site it may also need planning approval (the two are different — see the next question).

    A very short list of minor structures don't require building approval under Schedule 2 of the Queensland Building Regulation 2021 — generally garden sheds under 10m², some retaining walls under 1m, and fences under 2m. These are called accepted development (self-assessable), meaning no permit is needed but you are still responsible for complying with the Building Code of Australia, boundary setbacks, and the Planning Scheme.

    Don't rely on a summary for your specific project. What counts as exempt depends on exact dimensions, location on the lot, overlays, and what's already on the property. If in doubt, call 1300 697 372 before spending money — getting this wrong can mean rebuilding.

    Reference: Building Regulation 2021 — Schedule 2.

  • What's the difference between building approval and planning approval?
    ⌄

    These sound similar but answer two different questions:

    • Planning approval asks: can this use happen on this land? It's about where the building goes, how it's sited, and whether the use fits the Planning Scheme. Rules include setbacks, road access, watercourse distances, overlays (flood, bushfire), and proximity to things like quarries or feedlots.
    • Building approval asks: is the structure itself safe and compliant? It's about how the building is constructed — footings, framing, bracing, tie-down, energy efficiency, smoke alarms, and all the engineering that makes the building safe.

    Many rural houses only need building approval, because the Planning Scheme already sets out acceptable outcomes — if your site ticks every box on the Building a House in the Rural Zone checklist, no planning approval is required and you can go straight to building approval.

    If your site doesn't tick every box (e.g. in the bushfire zone, or unable to meet setbacks), you'll need planning approval first — specifically a Material Change of Use (MCU) application — and then building approval.

  • How do I check whether my rural site is suitable before I spend money?
    ⌄

    Two free tools — used together — will tell you most of what you need to know before you talk to anyone.

    1. SDRC Online Mapping. Open the Online Mapping tool, search your address, and switch on the Planning module. This confirms your zone (Rural, Rural Residential, etc.) and shows overlays — flood hazard, bushfire hazard, water resource catchment, airport environs. Each overlay triggers extra requirements.
    2. Rural Zone Checklist. Work through the Building a House in the Rural Zone checklist. It covers the "acceptable outcomes" (AOs) — setbacks, slope, flood-free building pad, water storage, separation from quarries / feedlots / abattoirs, energy supply, and overlay-specific requirements.

    If you can tick every item on the checklist, you don't need planning approval — just building approval. If any item can't be ticked, the site is still buildable, but it will need a planning application (MCU) first. See the MCU question below.

    Rural Zone setbacks

    Rural setbacks also interact with watercourses, animal industries, slope, and the size of a flood-free building pad.   • What if my site doesn't meet the Rural Zone Checklist?
    ⌄

    You apply for a Material Change of Use (MCU) — a planning approval that assesses your proposal against the Planning Scheme's performance outcomes rather than the strict acceptable outcomes.

    MCU (Material Change of Use): a type of planning application for when a land use is starting, restarting, or intensifying. Applies here because rural houses that can't meet all the acceptable outcomes become code assessable — they need planning approval before building approval.

    If a performance outcome can be met another way, approval is typically granted with conditions. For example, if there's no constructed road, a condition will be to construct one. If there's a nearby quarry, conditions may require building orientation or materials that reduce dust/noise impact.

    What a simple MCU needs:

    • Application form (DA Form 1) and fees
    • Written landowner consent
    • Supporting information — how you'll design, construct and operate, plus a statement addressing the Planning Scheme
    • Specific details for any checklist item you don't comply with

    A simple MCU takes around 20 business days to decide. Longer if an information request is issued or a Referral Agency is involved (in relation to your application, Council may need information from the State Government), or the application requires impact assessment.

    Detailed guidance: Planning & Development.

    MCUs are technical. For anything beyond the simplest case, consider engaging a town planner — or book a Pre-Lodgement Meeting with Council ($300, 60 minutes) for tailored advice regarding your specific application. General enquiries are free, call 1300 697 372 and ask to speak to the duty planner.
  • What are the setback requirements?
    ⌄

    Setbacks — how far a building has to sit from each boundary — depend on the zone of your land and the class of building. For rural land, the two classes most homes need to know about are:

    • Class 1a — the main dwelling.
    • Class 10a — sheds, garages, and carports.

    In the Rural Zone, dwellings and outbuildings follow different rules:

    Zone Class 1a (dwellings) Class 10a (sheds, garages, carports)
    Rural Zone 60m from all boundaries if gravel road frontage OR 6m from front & 60m from side & rear (if bitumen frontage) (or planning approval required) 6m from front boundary, regardless of road surface. 1.5m side & rear boundaries (or siting concession required)

    Class 1a dwellings — the 60m setback from every boundary on a gravel road is the rule most people are surprised by. It reflects the Planning Scheme's intent to keep rural dwellings well clear of unconstructed roads and neighbouring rural operations. Bitumen frontage gives a shorter front setback (6m) but side and rear remain at 60m. If your block can't meet either pattern, planning approval is required (see the MCU question above).

    Class 10a outbuildings — sheds, garages and carports follow the same residential-style rule regardless of road surface. If you can't meet 6m front or 1.5m side/rear, a siting concession can be applied for as part of the building application.

    Other things that commonly affect where you can build on rural land include watercourse separations, distances from animal industries, slope, and the size of a flood-free building pad. These don't change the setback rule above, but they do narrow down where on the block the rule can be satisfied. Call Council's building team on 1300 697 372 to talk through your specific block before finalising a house position.

    Rural Residential zone setbacks aren't shown here — the rules differ slightly from Rural Zone. If your land is Rural Residential (confirm on the Online Mapping tool), call Council's building team on 1300 697 372 for the correct values.
  • Should I use a private certifier or Council for building approval?
    ⌄
    Building certifier: a QBCC-licensed professional who checks the building work against the Building Act 1975, the National Construction Code, the Queensland Development Code, and the relevant Australian Standards. They decide the application, arrange inspections, and issue the Form 21 Final Certificate at the end. You can hire a private certifier or use Council as your certifier — the choice is yours.

    Council as certifier: available for dwellings and extensions. You prepare and lodge the application yourself (or with your builder's help). Council handles the assessment and inspections. Usually cheaper overall, but more work for you.

    Private certifier: most larger projects go this route. The certifier prepares and lodges the application for you, subcontracts extra professionals as needed (engineers, surveyors, soil tests, energy efficiency report, BAL report), liaises with Council on your behalf, and conducts on-site inspections. Council fees are the same either way — the certifier charges their own professional fee on top.

    Many people find a private certifier is worth the fee — they can catch missing documents before lodgement and optimise the construction schedule to the certification process. As the applicant, it is your decision what will work best for you.

  • What documents do I need to lodge a building application for a new rural house?
    ⌄

    For a Class 1a dwelling (new house or extension), the full lodgement package is:

    • Completed DA Form 2 (the QLD Government building development application form)
    • Site Plan, Floor Plan, Elevations, and Structural Details
    • Footing / Slab Design with Form 15 signed by a Registered Professional Engineer of Queensland (RPEQ)
    • Soil Test Report
    • Energy Efficiency Report (houses must meet 6-Star)
    • Bushfire Attack Level (BAL) Report — if the site is in the Bushfire Hazard Overlay
    • QBCC Insurance Certificate if using a licensed builder and the work is $3,300 or more; or QBCC Owner Builder Permit if building yourself and the work is $11,000 or more
    • QLeave payment receipt if the build is valued at $150,000 or more (excl. GST)

    Council's Building Info Sheets list the full requirements. Incomplete applications are rejected, so it's worth checking the list twice before lodging.

    You cannot commence any building work until you have received the Decision Notice (Approval). Starting early — even clearing or pouring footings — creates unapproved work that has to be undone or retrospectively certified at your cost.
  • What if I'm building a shed first, then a house later?
    ⌄

    This is a common pathway on the Southern Downs — build a shed to store equipment in, then build the permanent house later when finances allow. Both stages still need proper approval, but the process is similar for both.

    A shed (Class 10a — sheds, garages, carports) requires its own building application with a smaller checklist of documents than a new dwelling:

    • Completed DA Form 2
    • Site Plan, Floor Plan, Elevations, and Structural Details
    • Footing / Slab Design with Form 15 signed by an RPEQ, and a soil investigation report (unless the footing design is generic and suits all soil types)
    • Form 15 for the steel frame (most rural sheds)
    • QBCC Insurance Certificate or Owner Builder Permit as above
    • QLeave receipt if the shed is valued at $150,000 or more (excl. GST)

    Key differences from a Class 1a house application: a shed doesn't need an energy efficiency report. It still needs a BAL report if in the Bushfire Hazard Overlay and located within 6m of a residence.

    When you later apply for the house, it's a separate Class 1a application with the full document list. The shed doesn't "count" toward the house approval — they're assessed independently.

  • What about tiny homes, prefabricated houses, or shipping container builds?
    ⌄

    Short answer: a smaller or alternative building does not mean less approval. The most common misconception is that "tiny" or "prefab" means the normal rules don't apply. They do.

    Tiny homes

    Fixed to the land. Whether prefabricated or built on site, a tiny home permanently fixed to the land and used as a primary or secondary home is treated like any other Class 1a dwelling. Same building approval process, same document list, same inspections, same Form 21 at the end. Council removed the minimum 60m² floor area requirement in August 2024, so there's no "too small" threshold for a Class 1 dwelling in the Southern Downs.

    On wheels. Treated like caravans. They can be stored on the land without approval if they're genuinely just being stored (not lived in, not connected to services). Living in one on your land is only permitted under a Temporary Home permit (see the question below about living on the land during construction).

    Prefabricated and manufactured homes

    Can be approved as Class 1a dwellings, but the paperwork is often harder — not easier. Many commercially sold tiny homes and modular units are imported and may not meet Australian standards. Before buying, ask the supplier for written certification that building, plumbing and electrical works meet Queensland requirements, including RPEQ signoff. Things a certifier will want to verify include structural adequacy, ceiling height, energy efficiency (7-Star rating), termite barriers, damp and weatherproofing, fire safety, Form 15 for structural elements, Form 43 for waterproofing, and the ability to get QBCC insurance. If these certifications can't be produced, a building approval may not be obtainable — and without one, a Form 21 can't be issued.

    Shipping containers

    Depends entirely on the intended use:

    • Self-assessable (no approval): temporary short-term storage (under 6 months) on residential land during renovations or removals, provided it's not fixed to the ground, not on a road or road reserve, and doesn't affect community amenity. Also self-assessable for short-term storage on industrial, rural, or active construction sites.
    • Building approval required: any use as a building (house, secondary dwelling, bedroom, office, shed, workshop, hobby room); any use as a structure (swimming pool, fence, viewing platform, architectural feature); long-term storage (over 6 months); anchored to the ground with footings, slabs, electricity or plumbing; or altered with doors, windows, awnings, or attachments.
    • Amenity & Aesthetics application: required before building approval when the container is in any Urban or Rural Residential zone, or in the Rural zone if within 100m of a residential area, on a lot 4,000m² or less, or within 20m of a property boundary. Conditions commonly imposed include painting, concrete slab or 400mm stumps (to prevent rabbit harbourage — a Darling Downs Moreton Rabbit Board requirement), and sometimes a pitched roof.

    Council has dedicated fact sheets on both tiny houses and shipping containers. Given how specific the rules are to each situation, please call Council on 1300 697 372 before committing funds — a 10-minute conversation can save thousands.

  • What is a Form 15, Form 16, and Form 21?
    ⌄

    These are standard certificates used throughout the Queensland building process. You'll see them mentioned constantly:

    Form 15 — Compliance Certificate for Building Design or Specification. Signed by an RPEQ (registered engineer) to confirm that part of the design meets code. Commonly provided for footings, slabs, and steel framing. Required before building approval can be issued.
    Form 16 — Inspection Certificate / Aspect Certificate. Signed by the building certifier (or another competent person) after each stage of construction to confirm that stage passes. You'll collect four of these over the build — footing, slab, frame, final.
    Form 21 — Final Inspection Certificate. Issued by the certifier once the final inspection passes and all conditions of approval are met. It's the document that says the building is complete and lawful. For new dwellings, you cannot move in until Form 21 is issued.

    Your certifier manages all of these. Keep copies — they're important when you sell the house or change insurance.

  • What inspections happen during construction?
    ⌄

    For a Class 1a dwelling, the certifier must inspect and sign off at four stages. Each stage must pass before the next commences:

    1. Footing / Foundation — before concrete is poured. Confirms excavation depth, reinforcement, and placement match the engineer's design.
    2. Slab — after slab prep, before the pour. Confirms reinforcement, plumbing penetrations, and setout.
    3. Frame — once framing (walls, roof structure) is up and before sheeting goes on. Confirms tie-down, bracing, and member sizes. This is often where problems are caught.
    4. Final — at completion. Everything: stairs, balustrades, smoke alarms, waterproofing, energy efficiency, safety, and all conditions of approval.

    Your builder arranges inspections directly with your certifier. Plumbing has its own separate inspection schedule managed by Council plumbing inspectors — your plumber handles those bookings.

    If a stage fails, the certifier issues a written notice of what needs to be rectified. The work must be corrected and re-inspected before construction continues. If a licensed builder refuses to rectify defective work, the QBCC can issue a formal Direction to Rectify — see the QBCC.

  • When can I actually move in?
    ⌄

    For a new dwelling, you can move in once the Form 21 Final Certificate has been issued. Not before — even if the house feels finished.

    The Form 21 is only issued after the final inspection passes and all conditions of approval are satisfied. Common last-minute holdups: smoke alarm interconnection, balustrade compliance on decks over 1m, final stormwater discharge, and landscaping required by approval conditions.

    Occupying a new dwelling before Form 21 is an offence under the Building Act 1975. It can also affect your insurance.
  • Can I live on the land in a caravan or shed while I build?
    ⌄

    On rural land, yes — but only with Council approval, and only after you've received building approval for the permanent dwelling. It is not automatic, and it is not allowed in urban residential areas.

    The process:

    1. Obtain building approval for the dwelling first.
    2. Complete an Application for Permission to Occupy Temporary Accommodation and submit it to Council at least 30 days before your intended start date.
    3. Pay the applicable fee.
    4. Supply supporting information: drawings of the proposed temporary home, wastewater disposal plan, drinking water supply (minimum 5,000L tank required), landowner consent if you're not the owner, and a clear reason for needing temporary occupation.

    Approval can be issued for up to 18 months, and Council may extend it to match the expected completion of the permanent dwelling. The temporary accommodation approval lapses automatically once the dwelling is finished, the building approval lapses, or the approval term ends.

    Tents and camper trailers are not considered suitable temporary homes. A shed (Class 10a with prior building approval), caravan, or mobile home is. Full detail: SDRC — Temporary Homes.

    Don't start living on site before approval is granted. It can void the temporary approval entirely and create enforcement issues.
  • How do I arrange rubbish collection once I've moved in?
    ⌄

    Kerbside collection in the Southern Downs is contracted to Cleanaway. To start a residential service on a new dwelling, Cleanaway requires a Form 21 Final Certificate as proof the dwelling is complete and lawfully occupied. Until Form 21 is issued, a new property can't be added to the collection round.

    The garbage truck doesn't go everywhere. Many rural properties fall outside the Designated Waste Collection Area, so kerbside pickup isn't available. In those areas, Council operates rural bin compounds — shared, gated facilities where ratepayers can drop off their own waste. Residents living in a compound catchment can apply to Council for a key. Call 1300 697 372 to check whether your property is in a compound catchment and to request a key.

    Private collection. If your property is outside both kerbside coverage and a bin compound catchment, several private waste operators service rural properties in the region. Ask around locally, or call Council for a starting point.

  • Where do I get water if my tanks run low?
    ⌄

    Many bulk water carting businesses operate across the Southern Downs region and can deliver to rural tanks. Council maintains a list of approved water carters on the Approved Water Carters page.

    If you're arranging a delivery and want to confirm that a carter is licensed and approved to draw from Council's filling stations, call SDRC on 1300 697 372 and staff can confirm approval status.

    Planning your water storage from the start saves money on carting over time. Rural dwellings are required to have on-site storage of 45,000L for 1–2 bedrooms, 67,500L for more — these minimums exist to get you through a normal dry spell without needing deliveries.
  • What about sewerage? Do I need a septic tank or a treatment plant?
    ⌄

    Most rural properties are not connected to Council's sewerage network, so you'll need an On-Site Sewerage Facility (OSSF) to treat wastewater on your own land. There are two main options:

    • Septic tank with an absorption trench — the traditional approach. Wastewater flows into the tank, solids settle, and liquid effluent disperses through trenches into the soil. Works well on well-draining land with adequate room for the trenches.
    • Aerated Wastewater Treatment System (AWTS) — treats wastewater to a higher standard using aeration, which allows treated water to be dispersed over a smaller area, often via surface irrigation. Costs more to install and requires ongoing servicing, but is often the only option on smaller or difficult sites.

    Which is right for your block depends on soil type, lot size, slope, separation from watercourses, bores and neighbours, and the number of bedrooms. A soil test and a consultation with a licensed plumber are the two things that will give you a clear answer. Don't commit to a house design before this — the sewerage solution can influence building footprint and siting.

    OSSF installations require Council plumbing approval before work begins, and inspections during construction. Full detail and application forms: On-Site Sewerage Facilities (OSSF) — SDRC.

  • Do I need approval for my driveway?
    ⌄

    Yes — almost certainly. The issue is that any new or upgraded driveway has to cross Council land (the road reserve — the strip between your property boundary and the road itself) to reach the road, and Section 75(2) of the Local Government Act prohibits carrying out works on a road without Council's permission. That permission is called a Works Within Road Reserve approval. It's separate from, and in addition to, your building approval.

    Road reserve: the full width of land set aside for a road, including the sealed or gravelled surface, any verge or shoulder, table drains, and the strip to the property boundary. It's all Council-managed land, even though the verge and table drain may look like part of your property.

    When you need a Works Within Road Reserve approval:

    • New driveway access (part of virtually every new build)
    • Widening or relocating an existing driveway
    • Changing the surface type (e.g. gravel to concrete)
    • Adding a second access point, culvert, or kerb crossing

    Simple resealing or maintenance of an existing surface doesn't normally trigger an approval.

    What's involved:

    • Complete the Application for Minor Works — Works Within a Road Reserve form from Council's Engineering Department
    • Attach a sketch plan showing the proposed location, dimensions, and any culverts or drainage
    • Pay the application fee — see the current Fees & Charges schedule
    • Allow 20 working days for assessment
    • On approval, Council issues an Approval Letter setting out the conditions under which work can proceed

    Rural-specific considerations:

    • Longer driveways often need a culvert where the driveway crosses the table drain, so water can continue to flow. Culvert size and placement are part of the approval assessment.
    • Gravel driveways need approval too — the rule is about works on the road reserve, not about whether the driveway is sealed.
    • If your property accesses the New England Highway, Cunningham Highway, or another state-controlled road, driveway access is assessed by TMR, not Council — call TMR on 13 20 80 before applying to Council.

    Water or sewer infrastructure. If your driveway will pass over, or within 2m of, any Council water main, sewer main, or manhole, you'll also need a separate Works Near Water Supply or Sewerage Infrastructure approval under Section 192(2) of the Water Supply (Safety and Reliability) Act 2008. This is a second form, a second assessment, and a second fee. Council's Engineering Department will identify during your initial enquiry whether this applies.

    Most licensed driveway contractors will handle both approvals as part of their quote — but ask before signing rather than assuming. Call Council's Engineering Department on 1300 697 372 for pre-application advice before any concrete is poured.
  • How many animals can I keep on a rural block?
    ⌄

    Rural zoned land generally allows a broader range and number of animals than residential land — horses, cattle, sheep, goats, alpacas, poultry, and similar are all typical. There are still limits, though, and the limits depend on what type of animal, your lot size, and your zone.

    The Keeping of Animals Fact Sheet sets out the permitted numbers by animal type and zone. It's the definitive source — everything from dogs and cats, to horses, cattle, poultry, bees, pigs, and exotic animals is covered, with the maximum you can keep without a permit for each.

    If you want to keep more than the standard number, you'll need a Keeping of Animals permit. Call Council on 1300 697 372 to discuss your situation before applying — the waste team can advise whether a permit is likely to be approved for your block before you put an application in.

    Some breeds and species are restricted or prohibited regardless of lot size (restricted breed dogs, some reptiles, feral animals). Check the fact sheet before buying, breeding, or relocating animals to a new property.
  • What costs and fees should I budget for beyond construction?
    ⌄

    Beyond the construction itself, these are the items to keep in mind when budgeting for a new rural home. For current dollar figures, request quotes from the relevant providers and check the current SDRC Fees & Charges schedule.

    • Council building application fee — refer to the current SDRC Fees & Charges.
    • Private certifier fee, if using one.
    • Engineer (RPEQ) — for footing/slab design and Form 15, plus soil test.
    • Energy efficiency assessment — required for all new dwellings.
    • BAL assessment — only if in the Bushfire Hazard Overlay.
    • QBCC Home Warranty Insurance — required for residential work over $3,300. Paid by the builder and added to the contract price.
    • QLeave levy — required for work over $150,000 (excl. GST). Must be paid before building approval is issued.
    • Planning application fee — only if your site needs an MCU. Separate from building fees.
    • Pre-lodgement meeting — optional, $300 for 60 minutes with Council planners for tailored advice.
    • Infrastructure costs — rural sites often need driveway construction, stormwater management, on-site water storage (45,000L for 1–2 bedrooms, 67,500L for more), and on-site sewage treatment. Get quotes early.
  • I'm overwhelmed. Where do I start?
    ⌄

    Totally normal feeling. Here's the shortest sensible first week:

    1. Open the Online Mapping tool and confirm your property is planning zoned as rural. Then work through the Rural Zone Checklist to determine if your property requires planning approval. Mark each item ✓ / ✗ / NA.
    2. If any item is ✗, call Council on 1300 697 372 and ask to speak to the duty planner (general enquiries are free). Have your address, your ticks, and any ✗ items ready to discuss.
    3. Based on that call, you will know if you require planning approval to build on your property. If not using a private planning consultant, consider booking a Pre-Lodgement Meeting with Council's Town Planners.
    4. Get 2–3 quotes from licensed builders to establish a rough idea of what kind of build is possible within your budget and to get an idea of the possible timeline. If going that route, this is a good time to engage a private certifier.
    5. If you're not using a private certifier, consider booking a Pre-Lodgement Meeting with Council's building certifier to assist with preparing your building application and to discuss the necessary standards for your build to pass inspection when construction begins.

    At every step, Council staff are happy to help you understand the process — it's not a test, and you don't have to know all the terminology before you ring. That's what the duty planner is there for.

    • Planning approval asks: can this use happen on this land? It's about where the building goes, how it's sited, and whether the use fits the Planning Scheme.     If you want to build closer to a boundary than the standard setback allows, add a second dwelling, or the site has an overlay, you may need planning approval first — and then building approval.

  • How do I check if my block has any constraints?
    ⌄

    Open the SDRC Online Mapping tool, search your address, and switch on the Planning module. This confirms your zone and shows any overlays — the most common ones in town are:

    • Flood hazard — affects minimum floor levels and may trigger engineering assessment
    • Bushfire hazard — less common in the centres of Warwick and Stanthorpe, more common on the fringes and in smaller townships; may require a BAL (Bushfire Attack Level) report
    • Heritage or character — applies to some older parts of Warwick and Stanthorpe; may add design and materials requirements
    • Airport environs — height restrictions near the Warwick airport
    • Easements — sewer mains, water mains, and drainage easements commonly cross urban blocks and restrict where buildings can go. Check before siting.

    Each overlay triggers extra requirements. For specific advice on your block, call the duty planner on 1300 697 372 — general enquiries are free.

    ⌄

    Urban setbacks under the Queensland Development Code differ based on lot size:

    Lot size Class 1a (dwellings) Class 10a (sheds, garages, carports)
    450m² and over
    (QDC MP 1.2)    		 6m from front boundary, 1.5m from side & rear boundaries (or a siting concession is required) 1.5m from side & rear boundaries, subject to the QDC MP 1.2 A2(d) exemptions below for smaller outbuildings.
    Under 450m²
    (QDC MP 1.1)    		 Smaller front and side setbacks apply, scaled to the lot. Refer to the Queensland Housing Code for specific numbers. Scaled to lot — refer to QDC MP 1.1 / Queensland Housing Code, or call Council's building team.

    Common exemptions for lots 450m² and over under QDC MP 1.2:

    • QDC MP 1.2 A1(c) — Open Carports may be within the road setback if the perimeter of walls, solid screens, and supports within the setback is no more than 15% of the total, and there is no alternative on-site location that complies with normal setbacks, allows vehicle access at least 2.5m wide, and has a maximum gradient of 1 in 5.
    • QDC MP 1.2 A2(d) — Class 10a buildings (sheds, garages, carports) may be within side and rear boundary clearances where height is no more than 4.5m (or mean height no more than 3.5m), the total length of all buildings along any one boundary is no more than 9m, and they are no closer than 1.5m to a window in a habitable room of an adjoining dwelling.

    If your design can't meet standard setbacks and doesn't fit an exemption, a siting concession can be requested as part of your building application. The certifier assesses against the QDC's performance criteria, typically considering impact on streetscape, overshadowing, and privacy. A siting concession application adds time and cost to the process.

  • Should I use a private certifier or Council for building approval?
    ⌄
    Building certifier: a QBCC-licensed professional who checks the building work against the Building Act 1975, the National Construction Code, the Queensland Development Code, and the relevant Australian Standards. They decide the application, arrange inspections, and issue the Form 21 Final Certificate at the end. You can hire a private certifier or use Council as your certifier — the choice is yours.

    Council as certifier: available for dwellings and extensions. You prepare and lodge the application yourself (or with your builder's help). Council handles the assessment and inspections. Usually cheaper overall, but more work for you.

    Private certifier: the common route for urban dwellings and larger projects. The certifier prepares and lodges the application for you, subcontracts extra professionals as needed (engineers, surveyors, soil tests, energy efficiency report), liaises with Council on your behalf, and conducts on-site inspections. Council fees are the same either way — the certifier charges their own professional fee on top.

    Many people find a private certifier is worth the fee — they can catch missing documents before lodgement and optimise the construction schedule to the certification process. As the applicant, it is your decision what will work best for you.

  • What documents do I need to lodge a building application?
    ⌄

    For a Class 1a dwelling (new house or extension), the full lodgement package is:

    • Completed DA Form 2 (the Qld Government building development application form)
    • Site Plan, Floor Plan, Elevations, and Structural Details
    • Footing / Slab Design with Form 15 signed by a Registered Professional Engineer of Queensland (RPEQ)
    • Soil Test Report
    • Energy Efficiency Report (houses must meet the current energy standard)
    • BAL (Bushfire Attack Level) Report — only if the site is in the Bushfire Hazard Overlay
    • QBCC Insurance Certificate if using a licensed builder and the work is $3,300 or more; or QBCC Owner Builder Permit if building yourself and the work is $11,000 or more
    • QLeave payment receipt if the build is valued at $150,000 or more (excl. GST)

    Urban sites typically need fewer overlay-specific reports than rural — most blocks don't require a BAL report, bushfire protection measures, or extensive site-specific engineering beyond what's standard. Council's Building Info Sheets list the full requirements.

    You cannot commence any building work until you have received the Decision Notice (Approval). Starting early creates unapproved work that has to be undone or retrospectively certified at your cost.
  • What about extensions, decks, sheds, and carports?
    ⌄

    Rules of thumb for common urban projects (always confirm your specific project with a certifier):

    • Extensions — always need building approval. If the extension pushes into a setback, planning approval or a siting concession is also needed.
    • Decks and patios attached to the dwelling — need building approval. Decks higher than 1m above natural ground level need balustrades compliant with the Building Code.

  • Do I need approval for my driveway?
    ⌄

    Yes — almost certainly. Any new or upgraded driveway has to cross Council land (the road reserve, including the footpath and verge) to reach the road, and Section 75(2) of the Local Government Act prohibits carrying out works on a road without Council's permission. That permission is called a Works Within Road Reserve approval. It's separate from, and in addition to, your building approval.

    Road reserve: the full width of land set aside for a road, including the sealed surface, kerb and channel, footpath, and verge. It's all Council-managed land, even though the footpath and verge may look like part of your front garden.

    When you need a Works Within Road Reserve approval:

    • New driveway access (part of virtually every new build)
    • Widening or relocating an existing driveway
    • Changing the surface type (e.g. gravel to concrete)
    • Adding a second access point or a new kerb crossing

    Simple resealing or maintenance of an existing surface doesn't normally trigger an approval.

    What's involved:

    • Complete the Application for Minor Works — Works Within a Road Reserve form from Council's Engineering Department
    • Attach a sketch plan showing proposed location, dimensions, and any drainage
    • Pay the application fee — see the current Fees & Charges schedule
    • Allow 20 working days for assessment
    • On approval, Council issues an Approval Letter setting out the conditions under which work can proceed

    Watch for water and sewer infrastructure. On urban blocks, water mains, sewer mains, and manholes often run under footpaths and front yards. If your driveway will pass over, or within 2m of, any Council water or sewer infrastructure, you'll need a separate Works Near Water Supply or Sewerage Infrastructure approval under Section 192(2) of the Water Supply (Safety and Reliability) Act 2008 — a second form, a second assessment, and a second fee. Telltale signs of infrastructure below include meter boxes, sewer manholes, and valve or hydrant surface boxes on or near the verge. Council's Engineering Department will confirm what applies during your initial enquiry.

    Most licensed driveway contractors will handle both approvals as part of their quote — but ask before signing rather than assuming. Call Council's Engineering Department on 1300 697 372 for pre-application advice before any concrete is poured.
  • What about swimming pools?
    ⌄

    Any pool capable of holding water deeper than 300mm requires both building approval and compliant pool fencing. This includes permanent and most portable/inflatable pools, spas, and portable wading pools that meet the depth threshold.

    Pool safety in Queensland is governed by the Building Act 1975 and the Queensland Pool Safety Standard. Key points:

    • The pool must be registered on the Queensland Pool Safety Register.
    • A Pool Safety Certificate is required before selling or leasing a property with a pool, and whenever the fence is replaced or modified.
    • Pool fence inspections are performed by a licensed Pool Safety Inspector, not Council.
    • Council can investigate non-compliant or unfenced pools as a safety matter.

    Full guidance, including pool fence specifications, non-compliance reporting, and registration: Swimming Pools — SDRC.

    Child drownings remain the leading cause of accidental death for Queensland children under 5. Pool fencing requirements are strictly enforced and there's no discretion on the standard.
  • Can I build a granny flat or secondary dwelling?
    ⌄
    The Planning Scheme rules for secondary dwellings are currently being reviewed and may change. Please call Council on 1300 697 372 and speak with the duty planner before committing to a design or contract. General advice is confirmed below, but specific conditions will depend on the current scheme at the time you lodge.

    A secondary dwelling (often called a granny flat) is a second, smaller self-contained dwelling on the same lot as an existing house. In urban and rural residential zones, secondary dwellings generally require planning approval (MCU), plus building approval.

  • What is a Form 15, Form 16, and Form 21?
    ⌄

    These are standard certificates used throughout the Queensland building process:

    Form 15 — Compliance Certificate for Building Design or Specification. Signed by an RPEQ (registered engineer) to confirm that part of the design meets code. Commonly provided for footings, slabs, and steel framing. Required before building approval can be issued.
    Form 16 — Inspection Certificate / Aspect Certificate. Signed by the building certifier (or another competent person) after each stage of construction to confirm that stage passes. You'll collect four of these over the build — footing, slab, frame, final.
    Form 21 — Final Inspection Certificate. Issued by the certifier once the final inspection passes and all conditions of approval are met. It's the document that says the building is complete and lawful. For new dwellings, you cannot move in until Form 21 is issued.

    Your certifier manages all of these. Keep copies — they're important when you sell the house or change insurance.

  • What inspections happen during construction?
    ⌄

    For a Class 1a dwelling, the certifier must inspect and sign off at four stages. Each must pass before the next commences:

    1. Footing / Foundation — before concrete is poured.
    2. Slab — after slab prep, before the pour.
    3. Frame — once framing is up and before sheeting goes on.
    4. Final — at completion, including stairs, balustrades, smoke alarms, waterproofing, and all conditions of approval.

    Your builder arranges inspections with your certifier. Plumbing inspections are a separate schedule managed by Council plumbing inspectors. If a stage fails, the certifier issues a written notice of what needs to be rectified before construction continues.

  • When can I move in?
    ⌄

    For a new dwelling, once the Form 21 Final Certificate has been issued. Not before — even if the house looks finished.

    The Form 21 is only issued after the final inspection passes and all conditions of approval are satisfied. Common last-minute holdups: smoke alarm interconnection, balustrade compliance on decks over 1m, final stormwater discharge, and any landscaping required by approval conditions.

    Occupying a new dwelling before Form 21 is an offence under the Building Act 1975, and can affect your insurance.

    Once Form 21 is issued, you can also begin kerbside bin collection — see the Waste & Recycling FAQ for how to arrange.

  • I'm overwhelmed. Where do I start?
    ⌄

    Normal feeling. The shortest sensible first week:

    1. Check your zone and overlays on the Online Mapping tool. Most standard urban blocks fall within the Planning Scheme's acceptable outcomes, but overlays (flood, bushfire, heritage, character) will add requirements.
    2. If anything comes up that isn't straightforward — setback concessions, secondary dwelling, overlay-affected block — call Council on 1300 697 372 and ask to speak to the duty planner (general enquiries are free). Have your address and a rough description of what you want to build ready.
    3. Based on that call, you will know whether your build fits within the acceptable outcomes or whether planning approval is required. If planning approval is needed and you're not using a private planning consultant, consider booking a Pre-Lodgement Meeting with Council's Town Planners.
    4. Get 2–3 quotes from licensed builders to establish a rough idea of what kind of build is possible within your budget and to get an idea of the possible timeline. If going that route, this is a good time to engage a private certifier.
    5. If you're not using a private certifier, consider booking a Pre-Lodgement Meeting with Council's building certifier to assist with preparing your building application and to discuss the necessary standards for your build to pass inspection when construction begins.

    At every step, Council staff are happy to help you understand the process — it's not a test, and you don't have to know all the terminology before you ring. That's what the duty planner is there for.

  • I've been told I have a water leak. How do I find it?
    ⌄

  • My neighbour's tree drops branches, blocks light, or damages my fence. What can I do?
    ⌄

    Tree disputes between neighbours are a private matter that Council cannot mediate. They fall under the Queensland Neighbourhood Disputes (Dividing Fences and Trees) Act 2011.

    Practical steps:

    1. Talk to your neighbour first. Most tree issues are resolved when the owner is made aware.
    2. If you can't reach an agreement, you may serve a formal notice on the tree owner under the Act requesting action.
    3. If that doesn't work, the matter can be taken to QCAT: qcat.qld.gov.au or 1300 753 228.

    You have a right to trim branches that overhang your property up to the boundary line, at your own cost, provided you don't damage the tree's health. Return the trimmings to the tree owner (don't dump them).

    Trees on Council land or road reserves are a different matter — lodge a service request and a Council officer will assess. Never trim or remove a Council tree yourself.
  • I have a boundary or fencing dispute with my neighbour. What now?
    ⌄

    Fencing between two residential properties is governed by the Neighbourhood Disputes (Dividing Fences and Trees) Act 2011 — not Council. The general rule is that both neighbours share the cost of a "sufficient dividing fence" equally, but what counts as sufficient depends on the zone and existing fence.

    Practical steps:

    1. Talk to your neighbour first. Agree on what needs doing and get a quote from a fencing contractor.
    2. If you can't agree, serve a formal Notice to Fence on your neighbour. Templates are available from the Queensland Government.
    3. If there's still no agreement 30 days after the notice, either party can apply to QCAT for a decision: qcat.qld.gov.au or 1300 753 228.

    Boundary disputes (where the fence should sit) are separate from fencing disputes (who pays for what). If you're uncertain where the boundary actually is, a licensed surveyor can confirm. Council's online mapping shows cadastral boundaries for reference, but is not a substitute for a survey.

  • My neighbour's shed or extension looks unapproved. How do I report it?
    ⌄

    Not every structure requires approval. Small sheds (under 10m²), fences under 2m, and retaining walls under 1m may be self-assessable. See the Building Rural or Building Urban FAQ for the specifics.

Others require planning approval or simply aren't permitted in residential zones.

Dealing with weeds on your property? This FAQ covers the plants declared as pests in the Southern Downs, your legal obligations as a landholder, and the practical help Council can offer — including free or low-cost equipment hire.

  • Do I have to control weeds on my property?
    ⌄

    Generally, yes — but the obligation is more practical than most people expect. Queensland law asks every landholder to take "reasonable and practical" steps to prevent the spread of invasive plants from their land. That's the standard, rather than a requirement to eradicate every weed.

    What "reasonable and practical" looks like depends on the species, the size of the infestation, the size of the property, and what the landholder can realistically manage. Treating a small new outbreak of African Boxthorn is reasonable; hand-pulling every blackberry on 200 hectares of bushland is not.

    A few species do come with stronger obligations:

    • Blackberry, African Boxthorn, and Lantana are restricted invasive plants under the state Biosecurity Act 2014 — they must not be sold, given away, or released into the environment, and active control is expected.
    • Firethorn, Green Cestrum, and St John's Wort are declared pest plants under Council's local law, which gives Council the authority to take further action if needed.

    Council's Local Laws Team can help you work out what's reasonable for your property — call 1300 697 372.

  • Which weeds are a problem in the Southern Downs?
    ⌄

    A handful of species cause most of the grief locally. Each has a Queensland Government fact sheet with identification photos, life-cycle information, and detailed herbicide recommendations.

    Restricted invasive plants under the Biosecurity Act 2014 (statewide — cannot be sold, given away, or released):

    • African Boxthorn — spiny shrub, forms impenetrable thickets, harbours rabbits and foxes. A Weed of National Significance.
    • Blackberry — dense thickets, invades pastures and bushland, found around Stanthorpe, Warwick, and Killarney. A Weed of National Significance.
    • Lantana — common on recently purchased or neglected land, smothers native vegetation, toxic to stock.

    Locally declared under Council's Subordinate Local Law No. 7 (Council can issue control notices):

    • Firethorn — spiny ornamental that has escaped gardens; common on the Granite Belt between Warwick and the border.
    • Green Cestrum — toxic to cattle, sheep, horses, pigs, and poultry; spreads from roots and by birds.
    • St John's Wort — causes photosensitivity in grazing stock; known populations around Stanthorpe.

    Other priority species:

    • Tree Pear and other invasive cacti — Council offers free Cactus Injectors for landholders to treat on their own property (see equipment hire question below).

  • What herbicide should I use?
    ⌄

    Every Queensland Government weed fact sheet includes a detailed herbicide table — with specific products, application rates, situations (pasture, non-crop, native conservation area), and the right season to spray. These are the authoritative source and worth printing before you head to the rural supplies store.

    Queensland Government — Invasive plants fact sheets.

    A few general principles:

    • Always read the herbicide label and apply strictly in accordance with it. Labels are legally binding.
    • Check for permits. Some herbicide uses rely on APVMA minor-use permits (for example, firethorn is covered by permit PER11463). The relevant permit will be noted in the fact sheet.
    • Timing matters. Most weeds are best treated when actively growing — typically spring through autumn. Spraying dormant or stressed plants wastes chemical and gives poor results.
    • Remove grazing stock from sprayed areas during and after treatment — treated plants can become more palatable or temporarily more toxic.
    • Some properties are in hazardous chemical areas under the Agricultural Chemicals Distribution Control Act 1966, which restricts certain herbicides. Check before applying.

  • What's my legal responsibility for pest animals on my property?
    ⌄

    Every landholder in Queensland has a general biosecurity obligation (GBO) under the Biosecurity Act 2014 — the same law that underpins weed management. In plain terms: if an invasive animal is on your land and there are reasonable, practical steps you could take to limit the damage it does or its spread, you're expected to take them.

    General Biosecurity Obligation (GBO): Take all reasonable and practical measures to minimise the biosecurity risk posed by invasive animals under your control. What counts as "reasonable and practical" depends on your situation — a small lifestyle block, a large grazing property, or a suburban home — so there's no one-size-fits-all checklist.

    Several species are also formally declared as restricted invasive animals, meaning they must not be moved, kept, fed, given away, sold, or released. For Southern Downs landholders, the ones that come up most often are:

    • Wild dog and dingo (Canis familiaris / Canis familiaris dingo)
    • European red fox (Vulpes vulpes)
    • Feral pig (Sus scrofa)
    • Feral cat (Felis catus) — meaning an unowned cat
    • Rabbit (Oryctolagus cuniculus) — both wild and domestic varieties
    • Feral deer — primarily fallow deer (Dama dama) in the Southern Downs

    Indian mynas and some other urban pests are not declared restricted species, but still fall under the GBO and can be managed by landholders — see the Indian myna question below.

    For the authoritative species list and control advice, see the Queensland Government — Invasive animals page, or call Council's Local Laws Team on 1300 697 372 for advice specific to your property.

  • Wild dogs are attacking my stock. What should I do?
    ⌄

    Wild dogs — which includes dingoes, dingo-cross animals, and roaming unowned domestic dogs — are one of the most significant pest animal issues for producers in the Southern Downs. If they're attacking your stock:

    1. Protect remaining stock. Move vulnerable animals — lambing ewes, calving cows, young stock — to paddocks closer to the house or into yards if possible.
    2. Record the evidence. Photograph carcasses, bite patterns, tracks, and scats before you move or dispose of anything. Note GPS locations if you can. This helps officers assess the situation and supports coordinated control.
    3. Report to Council and log the incident in FeralScan. Call 1300 697 372 or use the MySDRC online service request. The free FeralScan app (WildDogScan specifically) lets you log sightings, attacks, and control activity — the data helps Council and neighbours coordinate.
    4. Talk to your neighbours. Wild dogs cover big ranges. Control on one property alone rarely holds for long — the gap fills quickly from surrounding country. A coordinated approach across adjoining properties is the single most effective thing you can do.

    Control methods available to landholders include 1080 baiting (the most economical option over large areas — see the baiting question below), trapping with padded foot-hold traps, shooting, and exclusion fencing including cluster fencing arrangements with your neighbours. Livestock guardian animals such as maremma dogs and alpacas are also used successfully by some producers — though they require careful planning if trapping or baiting is also underway nearby.

    Council's Local Laws Team can advise on which combination of methods suits your property and help connect you with neighbouring landholders for coordinated programs. Call 1300 697 372.

  • Does Council run a 1080 baiting program? How do I get baits?
    ⌄

    Yes. Council is an authorised supplier of 1080 (sodium fluoroacetate) baits for wild dog and fox control. Two options are available to landholders:

    1. Buy pre-prepared manufactured baits directly from Council — currently $36 for a 10-pack of wild dog baits (April 2026, subject to change). Pickup is available from both the Warwick and Stanthorpe pounds.
    2. Bring your own fresh meat to Council to be injected with 1080 at either the Warwick or Stanthorpe pound. This is often preferred because wild dogs in a particular area may already be habituated to taking a specific meat source from that property.

    Before you bait, you must:

    • Attend a baiting briefing or sign the required acknowledgements — Council will guide you through this.
    • Notify all adjoining neighbours at least 72 hours in advance.
    • Erect warning signage at every entrance to your property before baiting begins and leave it in place for at least one month afterwards.
    • Keep working dogs muzzled or restrained, and remove livestock from baited paddocks in accordance with the product label.
    1080 is extremely toxic to dogs and there is no reliable antidote. Baits and poisoned carcasses remain toxic for a considerable time — if your working dog or family pet takes a bait or eats a poisoned animal, the outcome is usually fatal. Neighbour notification, signage, and correct bait placement are legal requirements, not guidelines.

    Timing: Many landholders bait twice a year — typically March–May (targeting breeding adults) and again September–November (targeting pups and juveniles). Coordinating with your neighbours so baits are out at roughly the same time dramatically improves results.

    Call Council's Local Laws Team on 1300 697 372 to arrange bait supply, or to discuss whether baiting is the right approach for your situation.

  • I've got feral pig damage on my property. What can I do?
    ⌄

    Feral pigs are a restricted invasive animal — they must not be kept, fed, or moved. On rural properties, the common signs are rooting damage to pasture and crops, wallows near water, damaged fences, and lambs or poultry going missing.

    Control options that actually work:

    • Trapping with a pig-specific trigger is the most accessible method for small and medium properties. Silo-style cage traps with a funnel entrance or a lift-bar trigger are selective — only pigs will activate them — and pre-feeding for several nights before setting the trigger dramatically improves catch rates.
    • 1080 baiting is the most effective broadscale option but carries the same notification, signage, and pre-feeding requirements as wild dog baiting. Fermented grain is a common and effective bait.
    • Fencing with sheep mesh held tight to the ground, ideally with an electrified offset wire, can protect high-value crops or small paddocks.
    • Ground and aerial shooting can be effective in low-density or open-country situations, and thermal imaging improves detection at night.

    Council hires a Hog Hopper. This is a species-specific bait delivery device with a weighted, hinged lid that pigs can lift with their snouts but native animals, macropods, and most livestock cannot. It makes 1080 pig baiting substantially safer by reducing non-target risk, and is particularly useful on properties with wildlife corridors or mixed grazing. Hire is free — a $100 refundable deposit applies, maximum hire period two weeks, deposit claimable within three months of return. Call 1300 697 372 to arrange.

    Pigs have large home ranges and breed rapidly — a control program on one property alone is rarely enough. Coordinate with your neighbours whenever you can.

    Feral pigs can carry diseases transmissible to humans — leptospirosis, brucellosis, Q fever, melioidosis, and others. Wear gloves and avoid direct contact with blood, urine, or faeces. Don't eat undercooked feral pig meat, and don't feed raw feral pig meat or offal to dogs (swine brucellosis can pass from pigs to dogs to humans).

    Council's Local Laws Team can discuss trap designs, arrange bait supply, and help coordinate control across adjoining properties. Call 1300 697 372.

  • I've got a rabbit problem. What should I do?
    ⌄

    Queensland is the only Australian state that maintains a designated rabbit-free zone, protected by a rabbit-proof fence and actively managed by the Darling Downs-Moreton Rabbit Board (DDMRB). The Southern Downs sits inside this zone — which is why rabbit sightings here are treated seriously.

    If you see a wild rabbit in the Southern Downs, report it promptly to the DDMRB on 07 4661 4076 or to Council on 1300 697 372. The DDMRB has specialist officers for rabbit control within the protected zone.

    Pet rabbits are illegal in Queensland. Domestic and wild rabbits are the same species and will interbreed if domestic rabbits escape. Keeping a pet rabbit — even a desexed one in a secure hutch — is not permitted. Permits are only issued for research or certain forms of public entertainment (e.g. magic shows, circuses).

    If you currently have a pet rabbit, do not release it into the wild. Options:

    • Surrender it to Council at the Warwick or Stanthorpe office.
    • Contact the Darling Downs-Moreton Rabbit Board on 07 4661 4076 to arrange collection.
    • Arrange for humane euthanasia through a veterinarian.

    Council and the DDMRB would rather help you resolve a pet rabbit situation than see one released — so get in touch, and no one will be unpleasant about it.

  • Are feral deer a problem in the Southern Downs?
    ⌄

    Yes — and the Southern Downs has the distinction of being home to the largest wild fallow deer herd in Queensland, centred on the Pikedale area south-west of Warwick. Fallow deer were first released on the Darling Downs around Westbrook and Warwick between 1870 and 1872, and the Pikedale herd is now broadly continuous with the large New England Tableland population across the NSW border. For most landholders in the region, deer are a quiet background fact of rural life more than a front-of-mind problem — but if they're damaging crops, pasture, or fences on your property, the following applies.

    Species. Fallow deer (Dama dama) is the species to know locally. Queensland also has established populations of red, chital, and rusa deer, but those herds are well north and east of the Southern Downs.

    Legal status. Feral fallow deer are a category 3, 4 and 6 restricted invasive animal under the Biosecurity Act 2014. They must not be moved, fed, given away, sold, or released. The same general biosecurity obligation (GBO) that applies to other invasive animals applies here — take reasonable and practical steps to minimise the biosecurity risk on land you control.

    Farmed deer and deer in game parks are treated separately and must be kept in deer-proof enclosures. If an enclosed deer escapes and establishes in the wild, the owner is responsible under the Act. Anyone keeping deer needs to register as a biosecurity entity.

    Control options for landholders:

    • Ground shooting is considered the most effective and humane method currently available — usually done at night from a vehicle with spotlights or thermal equipment, by trained personnel with appropriate firearms licences.
    • Helicopter shooting can be cost-effective across large or open properties but is a specialised operation that's best coordinated across multiple adjoining landholders.
    • Trapping is sometimes an option in peri-urban areas where shooting isn't practical — typically a trip-wire activated self-closing gate or clover trap. Traps must be checked frequently and animals promptly tranquilised or euthanased; animal welfare considerations are significant.
    • Exclusion fencing works but is expensive. A proper deer fence is around 2.1m high with heavy-duty strainers, 9m post spacing, and netting pegged to the ground. Usually only practical for protecting high-value crops or farmed enclosures rather than whole properties.
    • Engaging licensed recreational hunters is a common approach among local landholders. Think through access conditions, public liability insurance, and references before allowing anyone on your property.

    Coordination matters. Deer have large home ranges and move between properties freely. Control on a single property is usually eroded by in-migration from surrounding country — so coordinated effort across several adjoining landholders is much more effective than anyone working alone. Council's Local Laws Team and local Landcare groups can help connect landholders working on the same issue.

    For species identification, detailed control guidance, and the Queensland Feral Deer Management Strategy 2022–27, see Biosecurity Queensland — Invasive animals. To discuss deer on your property or in your area, call Council's Local Laws Team on 1300 697 372.

  • Indian mynas are nesting in my roof or making a mess. What can I do?
    ⌄

    Indian mynas are not a declared restricted species, but they're still covered by the general biosecurity obligation — and Council supports residents who want to control them. They compete aggressively for tree hollows, evict native parrots, and roof nests can introduce mites, lice, and a fire risk.

    Don't confuse Indian mynas with native noisy miners. They look superficially similar but noisy miners are native and protected. Quick ID: Indian mynas have a brown body and bright yellow legs; noisy miners are grey with flesh-coloured legs. Both have yellow bills and yellow eye patches. Destroying a noisy miner is a wildlife offence.

    What actually reduces Indian myna numbers:

    • Block access to nest sites in your roof and eaves. Cover gaps, clear dead palm fronds promptly, and bird-proof gutters. Check for trapped possums before sealing permanently.
    • Don't feed them, even accidentally. Bring pet food inside, feed livestock where mynas can't reach it, and seal compost and food scraps. Bird feeders set out for native species often become myna feeders instead.
    • Remove nests and eggs (wearing gloves). Indian myna eggs are a distinctive blue-turquoise colour — no native hollow-nesting bird lays eggs that colour, so if you see them in your roof, you've got mynas. Seal eggs and nest material in a plastic bag and put it in your wheelie bin.
    • Restore native habitat. Mynas prefer cleared, disturbed land; planting local natives and reducing lawn area makes your property less attractive to them and more welcoming for native species.

    Trapping is also possible but requires careful planning, access to humane euthanasia, and enough coordination with neighbours to actually make a dent in the local population. If you're interested in setting up or joining a community action group, the Indian Myna Action Group publishes detailed guidance. Council can advise on local considerations.
