Thinking about building on rural land? This guide walks through the process from choosing a site to moving in — in the order the decisions actually happen.

If this is your first time building, don't worry about knowing the terminology. Each section explains the terms as we go (Form 15, Form 21, MCU, certifier, BAL, and so on). When in doubt, call Council on 1300 697 372 and ask for the duty planner — general enquiries are free.

  • Do I need approval to build on my rural block at all?
    ⌄

    Almost certainly yes. A new house always needs building approval, and depending on the site it may also need planning approval (the two are different — see the next question).

    A very short list of minor structures don't require building approval under Schedule 2 of the Queensland Building Regulation 2021 — generally small sheds under 10m², some retaining walls under 1m, and fences under 2m. These are called accepted development (self-assessable), meaning no permit is needed but you are still responsible for complying with the Building Code of Australia, boundary setbacks, and the Planning Scheme.

    Don't rely on a summary for your specific project. What counts as exempt depends on exact dimensions, location on the lot, overlays, and what's already on the property. If in doubt, call 1300 697 372 before spending money — getting this wrong can mean rebuilding.

    Reference: Building Regulation 2021 — Schedule 2.

  • What's the difference between building approval and planning approval?
    ⌄

    These sound similar but answer two different questions:

    • Planning approval asks: can this use happen on this land? It's about where the building goes, how it's sited, and whether the use fits the Planning Scheme. Rules include setbacks, road access, watercourse distances, overlays (flood, bushfire), and proximity to things like quarries or feedlots.
    • Building approval asks: is the structure itself safe and compliant? It's about how the building is constructed — footings, framing, bracing, tie-down, energy efficiency, smoke alarms, and all the engineering that makes the building safe.

    Most rural houses only need building approval, because the Planning Scheme already sets out acceptable outcomes — if your site ticks every box on the Rural Zone Checklist, no planning approval is required and you can go straight to building approval.

    If your site doesn't tick every box (e.g. too close to a watercourse, no constructed road access), you'll need planning approval first — specifically a Material Change of Use (MCU) application — and then building approval.

    Short version: planning = can I build here?  ·  building = can I build it this way?
  • Why should I bother with approval? What are the actual benefits?
    ⌄

    Beyond being legally required, approval protects you in concrete ways:

    • Insurance. Most home and contents insurers will refuse claims on unapproved structures. A fire, flood, or storm could leave you with nothing.
    • Resale. Unapproved work is a red flag at sale — buyers' solicitors will flag it, banks may refuse finance, and you may have to retrospectively certify (often more expensive than doing it right the first time).
    • Safety. The inspection regime exists because unmonitored construction has a genuinely poor safety record. Footing, slab, frame, and final inspections catch mistakes while they're cheap to fix.
    • Warranty & recourse. Licensed builders are bound by QBCC standards. If work is defective, the QBCC can compel rectification — but only on approved work by licensed builders.
    • Enforcement. Council can order unapproved structures to be demolished, and fines are significant. Building without approval and getting caught mid-build is a genuinely bad day.
  • How do I check whether my rural site is suitable before I spend money?
    ⌄

    Two free tools — used together — will tell you most of what you need to know before you talk to anyone.

    1. SDRC Online Mapping. Open the Online Mapping tool, search your address, and switch on the Planning module. This confirms your zone (Rural, Rural Residential, etc.) and shows overlays — flood hazard, bushfire hazard, water resource catchment, airport environs. Each overlay triggers extra requirements.
    2. Rural Zone Checklist. Work through the Building a House in the Rural Zone checklist. It covers the "acceptable outcomes" (AOs) — setbacks, slope, flood-free building pad, water storage, separation from quarries / feedlots / abattoirs, energy supply, and overlay-specific requirements.

    If you can tick every item on the checklist, you don't need planning approval — just building approval. If any item can't be ticked, the site is still buildable, but it will need a planning application (MCU) first. See the MCU question below.

    Rural setbacks are much bigger than township setbacks. Key ones: 60m from side and rear boundaries; 60m from gravel/unmade road; 50m from a watercourse; 200m+ from various animal industries. A 17m × 17m flood-free, under-15%-slope building pad is required at natural ground level.
  • What if my site doesn't meet the Rural Zone Checklist?
    ⌄

    You apply for a Material Change of Use (MCU) — a planning approval that assesses your proposal against the Planning Scheme's performance outcomes rather than the strict acceptable outcomes.

    MCU (Material Change of Use): a type of planning application for when a land use is starting, restarting, or intensifying. Applies here because rural houses that can't meet all the acceptable outcomes become code assessable — they need planning approval before building approval.

    If a performance outcome can be met another way, approval is typically granted with conditions. For example, if there's no constructed road, a condition will be to construct one. If there's a nearby quarry, conditions may require building orientation or materials that reduce dust/noise impact.

    What a simple MCU needs:

    • Application form (DA Form 1) and fees
    • Written landowner consent
    • Supporting information — how you'll design, construct and operate, plus a statement addressing the Planning Scheme
    • Specific details for any checklist item you don't comply with

    A simple MCU takes around 20 business days to decide. Longer if an information request is issued, a Referral Agency is involved (e.g. State-controlled roads), or the application requires impact assessment.

    Detailed guidance: Planning & Development.

    MCUs are technical. For anything beyond the simplest case, consider engaging a town planner — or book a Pre-Lodgement Meeting with Council ($300, 60 minutes) for tailored advice before you lodge. General enquiries are free.
  • Should I use a private certifier or Council for building approval?
    ⌄
    Building certifier: a QBCC-licensed professional who checks the building work against the Building Act 1975, the National Construction Code, the Queensland Development Code, and the relevant Australian Standards. They decide the application, arrange inspections, and issue the Form 21 Final Certificate at the end. You can use either a private certifier or Council as your certifier — the choice is yours.

    Private certifier: most larger projects go this route. The certifier prepares and lodges the application for you, subcontracts extra professionals as needed (engineers, surveyors, soil tests, energy efficiency report, BAL report), liaises with Council on your behalf, and conducts on-site inspections. Council fees are the same either way — the certifier charges their own professional fee on top.

    Council as certifier: available for dwellings and extensions. You prepare and lodge the application yourself (or with your builder's help). Council handles the assessment and inspections. Usually cheaper overall, but more work for you.

    For a first-time owner-builder on a rural site, most people find a private certifier is worth the fee — they catch missing documents before lodgement and save time. Get a couple of quotes.
  • What documents do I need to lodge a building application for a new rural house?
    ⌄

    For a Class 1a dwelling (new house or extension), the full lodgement package is:

    • Completed DA Form 2 (the building development application form)
    • Site Plan, Floor Plan, Elevations, and Structural Details
    • Footing / Slab Design with Form 15 signed by a Registered Professional Engineer of Queensland (RPEQ)
    • Soil Test Report
    • Energy Efficiency Report (houses must meet 6-Star)
    • Bushfire Attack Level (BAL) Report — if the site is in the Bushfire Hazard Overlay
    • QBCC Insurance Certificate if using a licensed builder and the work is $3,300 or more; or QBCC Owner Builder Permit if building yourself and the work is $11,000 or more
    • QLeave payment receipt if the build is valued at $150,000 or more (excl. GST)

    Council's Building Info Sheets list the full requirements. Incomplete applications are rejected, so it's worth checking the list twice before lodging.

    You cannot commence any building work until you have received the Decision Notice (Approval). Starting early — even clearing or pouring footings — creates unapproved work that has to be undone or retrospectively certified at your cost.
  • What is a Form 15, Form 16, and Form 21?
    ⌄

    These are standard certificates used throughout the Queensland building process. You'll see them mentioned constantly:

    Form 15 — Compliance Certificate for Building Design or Specification. Signed by an RPEQ (registered engineer) to confirm that part of the design meets code. Commonly provided for footings, slabs, and steel framing. Required before building approval can be issued.
    Form 16 — Inspection Certificate / Aspect Certificate. Signed by the building certifier (or another competent person) after each stage of construction to confirm that stage passes. You'll collect four of these over the build — footing, slab, frame, final.
    Form 21 — Final Inspection Certificate. Issued by the certifier once the final inspection passes and all conditions of approval are met. It's the document that says the building is complete and lawful. For new dwellings, you cannot move in until Form 21 is issued.

    Your certifier manages all of these. Keep copies — they're important when you sell the house or change insurance.

  • What inspections happen during construction?
    ⌄

    For a Class 1a dwelling, the certifier must inspect and sign off at four stages. Each stage must pass before the next commences:

    1. Footing / Foundation — before concrete is poured. Confirms excavation depth, reinforcement, and placement match the engineer's design.
    2. Slab — after slab prep, before the pour. Confirms reinforcement, plumbing penetrations, and setout.
    3. Frame — once framing (walls, roof structure) is up and before sheeting goes on. Confirms tie-down, bracing, and member sizes. This is often where problems are caught.
    4. Final — at completion. Everything: stairs, balustrades, smoke alarms, waterproofing, energy efficiency, safety, and all conditions of approval.

    Your builder arranges inspections directly with your certifier. Plumbing has its own separate inspection schedule managed by Council plumbing inspectors — your plumber handles those bookings.

    If a stage fails, the certifier issues a written notice of what needs to be rectified. The work must be corrected and re-inspected before construction continues. If a licensed builder refuses to rectify defective work, the QBCC can issue a formal Direction to Rectify — see the QBCC.

  • When can I actually move in?
    ⌄

    For a new dwelling, you can move in once the Form 21 Final Certificate has been issued. Not before — even if the house feels finished.

    The Form 21 is only issued after the final inspection passes and all conditions of approval are satisfied. Common last-minute holdups: smoke alarm interconnection, balustrade compliance on decks over 1m, final stormwater discharge, and landscaping required by approval conditions.

    Occupying a new dwelling before Form 21 is an offence under the Building Act 1975. It can also affect your insurance.
  • Can I live on the land in a caravan or shed while I build?
    ⌄

    On rural land, yes — but only with Council approval, and only after you've received building approval for the permanent dwelling. It is not automatic, and it is not allowed in urban residential areas.

    The process:

    1. Obtain building approval for the dwelling first.
    2. Complete an Application for Permission to Occupy Temporary Accommodation and submit it to Council at least 30 days before your intended start date.
    3. Pay the applicable fee.
    4. Supply supporting information: drawings of the proposed temporary home, wastewater disposal plan, drinking water supply (minimum 5,000L tank required), landowner consent if you're not the owner, and a clear reason for needing temporary occupation.

    Approval can be issued for up to 18 months, and Council may extend it to match the expected completion of the permanent dwelling. The temporary accommodation approval lapses automatically once the dwelling is finished, the building approval lapses, or the approval term ends.

    Tents and camper trailers are not considered suitable temporary homes. A shed (Class 10a with prior building approval), caravan, or mobile home is. Full detail: SDRC — Temporary Homes.

    Don't start living on site before approval is granted. It can void the temporary approval entirely and create enforcement issues.
  • What costs and fees should I budget for beyond construction?
    ⌄

    Rough orders of magnitude — get specific quotes for your project. Figures are indicative only and change over time:

    • Council building application fee — set in the annual Fees & Charges schedule. Refer to the current SDRC Fees & Charges.
    • Private certifier fee (if using one) — commonly a few thousand dollars for a standard dwelling.
    • Engineer (RPEQ) — for footing/slab design and Form 15, plus soil test.
    • Energy efficiency assessment — required for all new dwellings.
    • BAL assessment — only if in the Bushfire Hazard Overlay.
    • QBCC Home Warranty Insurance — required for residential work over $3,300. Premium based on contract value. Paid by the builder and added to the contract price.
    • QLeave levy — required for work over $150,000 (excl. GST). A small percentage of contract value. Must be paid before building approval is issued.
    • Planning application fee — only if your site needs an MCU. Separate from building fees.
    • Pre-lodgement meeting — optional, $300 for 60 minutes with Council planners for tailored advice.
    • Infrastructure costs — rural sites often need driveway construction, stormwater management, on-site water storage (45,000L for 1–2 bedrooms, 67,500L for more), and on-site sewage treatment (typical costs are a significant multi-thousand-dollar item — get quotes early).
  • I'm overwhelmed. Where do I start?
    ⌄

    Totally normal feeling. Here's the shortest sensible first week:

    1. Open the Online Mapping tool and confirm your zone and overlays.
    2. Print and work through the Rural Zone Checklist. Mark each item ✓ / ✗ / NA.
    3. Call Council on 1300 697 372 and ask to speak to the duty planner (general enquiries are free). Have your address, your ticks, and any ✗ items ready.
    4. Based on that call, get 2–3 quotes from licensed builders and, if going that route, from a private certifier.
    5. If any item is ✗, consider booking a Pre-Lodgement Meeting before investing further.

    At every step, Council staff are happy to help you understand the process — it's not a test, and you don't have to know the terminology before you ring. That's what the duty planner is there for.

  • My neighbour's dog barks constantly. Can Council do something?
    ⌄

    Yes. Persistent barking is a noise nuisance Council can investigate under local laws. The process works in steps:

    1. Try direct contact first. In many cases the owner isn't aware — a friendly note often resolves it without Council involvement.
    2. Lodge a service request via Council's online service request form or by calling 1300 697 372.
    3. Keep a noise diary. Council will ask you to record dates, times, and how the barking affects you. This is essential — without it, Council has limited ability to take action.
    4. Council investigates. Once the diary is received, a Local Laws Officer will contact the dog owner.
    Download the Barking Dogs Fact Sheet and Noise Diary to get started.
  • Why does Council need a noise diary? Why can't an officer just come and listen?
    ⌄

    A noise diary creates an objective record showing a pattern — which Local Laws Officers need to demonstrate a genuine nuisance under the legislation. It also protects you by documenting the problem factually.

  • How do I register my dog?
    ⌄

    Download the Dog Registration Application Form and return it to Council. If applicable, also email proof of desexing or a pension card to mail@sdrc.qld.gov.au.

    Full details: Dog Registration — SDRC website.

  • How many animals am I allowed to keep on my property?
    ⌄

    Permitted numbers vary by property type and zone under local bylaws and the planning scheme. The Keeping of Animals Fact Sheet sets out permitted numbers by animal type.

    To keep more animals than permitted, a Keeping of Animals permit is required. Call 1300 697 372 to discuss your situation before applying.

  • My dog is missing or has been impounded. What do I do?
    ⌄

    Call Council on 1300 697 372 as soon as possible with your dog's breed, colour, size, and tag number. If impounded, a Local Laws Officer will advise the collection time, release fee, and whether registration is required before release.

    You can also check the Impounded and Rescued Animals page.

  • I've found a stray dog. What should I do?
    ⌄

    Call Council on 1300 697 372. A Local Laws Officer will attend to collect the animal. Please don't attempt to physically restrain an unknown dog — even a friendly animal can bite when stressed.

  • I've been attacked by a dog. What do I do?
    ⌄
    If anyone needs emergency medical attention, call 000 first.

    Once you're safe, call Council on 1300 697 372. A Local Laws Officer will attend the scene. You will also need to submit a Statement of Dog Attack form — email the completed form to mail@sdrc.qld.gov.au. When you call, have ready: the location, a description of the dog, whether it is still in the area, and the time of the attack.

  • I've been told I have a water leak. How do I find it?
    ⌄

    Many leaks can be found without a plumber. Check inside first — a running toilet is the most common cause. Add a few drops of food colouring to the cistern and wait 10 minutes without flushing; if colour appears in the bowl, the cistern is leaking. Also check all taps, showerheads, and washing machine connections.

    Outside, look for unusually green or boggy ground — this can indicate an underground pipe failure even when there's no visible surface water. Check garden timers and hose guns too.

    The Checking for Water Leaks Fact Sheet includes a method for testing underground pipes without a plumber.
  • There's a leak at the meter or on the footpath — is that Council's responsibility?
    ⌄

    Council's side (the meter itself, or the pipe between the water main and the meter): report to Council immediately on 1300 697 372.

    Your side (the pipe running from the meter into the property): this is the property owner's responsibility. Consider shutting off the water at the meter while you arrange a licensed plumber.

    Not sure which side? Call 1300 697 372 and we can help work it out.

  • Can I get a rates adjustment if I had an unexpected leak?
    ⌄

    In some circumstances Council may consider an adjustment for water lost to an undetected leak. Contact Council's Water Team on 1300 697 372 to discuss. Having your repair invoice available will assist with the assessment.

  • How can I monitor my water usage between bills?
    ⌄

    The My SDRC Water portal connects to your Smart Water Meter and lets you view near-real-time usage and set a custom alert threshold to catch a leak early. Sign-up details are on page 4 of your rates notice. See also: Smart Water Meters.

  • Where is my water meter?
    ⌄

    Water meters are usually near the front boundary of the property close to the street — often in a small covered box set into the ground. If you can't find yours, call 1300 697 372 and we can look up the exact location for your address.

  • My bin wasn't collected. What do I do?
    ⌄

    Lodge a request via Council's online service request form or call 1300 697 372. Cleanaway will schedule a return collection before your next regular collection day.

    Before you lodge: confirm the bin was at the kerbside by 6:00am and wasn't blocked by a parked vehicle — these are the most common reasons a bin is passed.
  • What day is my bin collected?
    ⌄

    Your collection day is tied to your property address. Search your address using SDRC's online mapping tool to find your bin day, or call 1300 697 372. Collection days may change on public holidays — Council will publish any changes in advance.

  • My bin is damaged or missing. Can I get a replacement?
    ⌄

    Yes. Lodge a request via Council's online service request form or call 1300 697 372. Include your address and describe the issue.

  • I'm elderly or have a disability and can't put my bins out. Is there help available?
    ⌄

    Yes. Council offers an assisted collection service for residents unable to put bins out due to age, disability, or medical immobility. Cleanaway drivers collect and return the bins as part of their normal round. To apply, email mail@sdrc.qld.gov.au with a letter requesting the service and a note or certificate from a registered health practitioner (subject line: ATTN Waste Team).

  • Where do I take items I can't put in the bin?
    ⌄

    Council's waste facilities accept household items, green waste, scrap metal, and regulated materials. Locations, opening hours, and fees are on the Residential Waste & Recycling page. For items like mattresses, whitegoods, and e-waste, check the fact sheet first — some are accepted free, others attract a fee.

    Asbestos: pre-booking is required. Call 1300 697 372 to arrange before attending.

  • How do I report a pothole or road damage?
    ⌄

    Report online using Council's online service request form — attach a screenshot with a map pin showing the exact location. Alternatively call 1300 697 372. Include the street name, nearest landmark, and an estimate of the size.

  • Is the damaged road Council's or the State Government's responsibility?
    ⌄

    Not all roads in the region are Council's responsibility. Major highways are managed by the Department of Transport and Main Roads (TMR).

    • State roads (New England Hwy, Cunningham Hwy, other TMR roads): contact TMR — 13 20 80
    • Rail crossings / railway tracks: contact Queensland Rail — 13 16 17
    • Council roads: lodge a request with Council as above

    Not sure? Call Council on 1300 697 372 and we can help.

  • How do I check if a road is closed?
    ⌄

    The SDRC Disaster Dashboard has current road closure information and flood camera images of key crossings — the most up-to-date source during weather events. For State roads check Queensland Traffic.

  • My vehicle was damaged by a road defect. Can I make a claim?
    ⌄

    You can submit a letter of demand to Council. Include your name, time and location of the incident, nature of the damage, and any supporting evidence. For road-related claims address to: ATTN: Governance, Southern Downs Regional Council, PO Box 26, Warwick QLD 4370, or email mail@sdrc.qld.gov.au.

  • What disputes can Council help with?
    ⌄

    Council's Local Laws team can investigate: persistent noise nuisances (barking dogs, loud machinery, amplified music); dust, smoke, odour, or light pollution; water pollution from a neighbouring property; unauthorised building works or illegal use of a structure; and unlawful land use.

    Lodge a complaint via Council's online service request form or call 1300 697 372.

  • What disputes are outside Council's jurisdiction?
    ⌄

    Some common disputes are private matters Council cannot mediate or enforce — including boundary and fencing disputes, trees crossing property lines, parking outside the Warwick CBD, and easement disputes. For these matters the Queensland Civil and Administrative Tribunal (QCAT) is often the right avenue: qcat.qld.gov.au or 1300 753 228.

  • There are weeds on a Council road near my property. Who do I contact?
    ⌄

    Council has a dedicated Invasive Pests program with officers responsible for weed control on Council roads and land. Report via Council's online service request form or call 1300 697 372.

    Weeds on the New England Highway or Cunningham Highway? These are State Government roads managed by TMR — contact TMR on 13 20 80.

  • How do I pay my rates?
    ⌄

    Rates can be paid online via SDRC Online Payments, by BPAY (biller code and reference on your notice), or in person at the Warwick or Stanthorpe office. You can also pay by post — cheque payable to Southern Downs Regional Council, PO Box 26, Warwick QLD 4370.

    ⌄

    Contact Council as early as possible on 1300 697 372. Options may include instalment arrangements, hardship provisions, or deferral in exceptional circumstances — the sooner you contact us, the more options are available. See the Payment Difficulties page for more information.

  • How do I receive my rates notice by email instead of post?
    ⌄

    Scan the QR code on page 2 of your rates notice to register for eNotices and receive future notices by email — faster, paperless, and you're notified as soon as your notice is ready.
