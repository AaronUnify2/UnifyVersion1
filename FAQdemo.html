Thinking about building on rural land? This guide walks through the process from choosing a property to moving in.

If this is your first time building, this FAQ covers common questions and explains terminology. For general advice relating to building approval, you can call Council on 1300 697 372 — general enquiries are free.

  • Do I need approval to build on my rural block?
    ⌄

    Almost certainly yes. A new house, shed, or carport needs building approval, and depending on the site it may also need planning approval (the two are different — see the next question).

    A very short list of minor structures don't require building approval under Schedule 2 of the Queensland Building Regulation 2021 — generally garden sheds under 10m², some retaining walls under 1m, and fences under 2m. These are called accepted development (self-assessable), meaning no permit is needed but you are still responsible for complying with the Building Code of Australia, boundary setbacks, and the Planning Scheme.

    Don't rely on a summary for your specific project. What counts as exempt depends on exact dimensions, location on the lot, overlays, and what's already on the property. If in doubt, call 1300 697 372 before spending money — getting this wrong can mean rebuilding.

    Reference: Building Regulation 2021 — Schedule 2.

  • What's the difference between building approval and planning approval?
    ⌄

    These sound similar but answer two different questions:

    • Planning approval asks: can this use happen on this land? It's about where the building goes, how it's sited, and whether the use fits the Planning Scheme. Rules include setbacks, road access, watercourse distances, overlays (flood, bushfire), and proximity to things like quarries or feedlots.
    • Building approval asks: is the structure itself safe and compliant? It's about how the building is constructed — footings, framing, bracing, tie-down, energy efficiency, smoke alarms, and all the engineering that makes the building safe.

    Many rural houses only need building approval, because the Planning Scheme already sets out acceptable outcomes — if your site ticks every box on the Building a House in the Rural Zone checklist, no planning approval is required and you can go straight to building approval.

    If your site doesn't tick every box (e.g. in the bushfire zone, or unable to meet setbacks), you'll need planning approval first — specifically a Material Change of Use (MCU) application — and then building approval.

  • How do I check whether my rural site is suitable before I spend money?
    ⌄

    Two free tools — used together — will tell you most of what you need to know before you talk to anyone.

    1. SDRC Online Mapping. Open the Online Mapping tool, search your address, and switch on the Planning module. This confirms your zone (Rural, Rural Residential, etc.) and shows overlays — flood hazard, bushfire hazard, water resource catchment, airport environs. Each overlay triggers extra requirements.
    2. Rural Zone Checklist. Work through the Building a House in the Rural Zone checklist. It covers the "acceptable outcomes" (AOs) — setbacks, slope, flood-free building pad, water storage, separation from quarries / feedlots / abattoirs, energy supply, and overlay-specific requirements.

    If you can tick every item on the checklist, you don't need planning approval — just building approval. If any item can't be ticked, the site is still buildable, but it will need a planning application (MCU) first. See the MCU question below.

    Setback comparison — Rural vs Residential / Rural Residential zones

    Zone Setback requirements
    Rural Zone 60m from all boundaries if dirt road frontage OR 6m from front & 60m from side & rear (if bitumen frontage) (or planning approval required)
    Residential / Rural Residential 6m from front boundary, regardless of road surface. 1.5m side & rear boundaries (or siting concession required)

    Other rural requirements include 50m from a watercourse, 200m+ from various animal industries, and a 17m × 17m flood-free building pad on slope under 15% at natural ground level.

    Common exemptions apply for single detached housing on lots 450m² and over, under the Queensland Development Code:

    • QDC MP 1.2 A1(c) — Open Carports may be within the road setback if the perimeter of walls, solid screens and supports within the setback is no more than 15% of the total, and there is no alternative on-site location that complies with normal setbacks, allows vehicle access at least 2.5m wide, and has a maximum gradient of 1 in 5.
    • QDC MP 1.2 A2(d) — Class 10a buildings (sheds, garages, carports) may be within side and rear boundary clearances where height is no more than 4.5m (or mean height no more than 3.5m), the total length of all buildings along any one boundary is no more than 9m, and they are no closer than 1.5m to a window in a habitable room of an adjoining dwelling.
  • What if my site doesn't meet the Rural Zone Checklist?
    ⌄

    You apply for a Material Change of Use (MCU) — a planning approval that assesses your proposal against the Planning Scheme's performance outcomes rather than the strict acceptable outcomes.

    MCU (Material Change of Use): a type of planning application for when a land use is starting, restarting, or intensifying. Applies here because rural houses that can't meet all the acceptable outcomes become code assessable — they need planning approval before building approval.

    If a performance outcome can be met another way, approval is typically granted with conditions. For example, if there's no constructed road, a condition will be to construct one. If there's a nearby quarry, conditions may require building orientation or materials that reduce dust/noise impact.

    What a simple MCU needs:

    • Application form (DA Form 1) and fees
    • Written landowner consent
    • Supporting information — how you'll design, construct and operate, plus a statement addressing the Planning Scheme
    • Specific details for any checklist item you don't comply with

    A simple MCU takes around 20 business days to decide. Longer if an information request is issued or a Referral Agency is involved (in relation to your application, Council may need information from the State Government), or the application requires impact assessment.

    Detailed guidance: Planning & Development.

    MCUs are technical. For anything beyond the simplest case, consider engaging a town planner — or book a Pre-Lodgement Meeting with Council ($300, 60 minutes) for tailored advice before you lodge. General enquiries are free.
  • Should I use a private certifier or Council for building approval?
    ⌄
    Building certifier: a QBCC-licensed professional who checks the building work against the Building Act 1975, the National Construction Code, the Queensland Development Code, and the relevant Australian Standards. They decide the application, arrange inspections, and issue the Form 21 Final Certificate at the end. You can hire a private certifier or use Council as your certifier — the choice is yours.

    Council as certifier: available for dwellings and extensions. You prepare and lodge the application yourself (or with your builder's help). Council handles the assessment and inspections. Usually cheaper overall, but more work for you.

    Private certifier: most larger projects go this route. The certifier prepares and lodges the application for you, subcontracts extra professionals as needed (engineers, surveyors, soil tests, energy efficiency report, BAL report), liaises with Council on your behalf, and conducts on-site inspections. Council fees are the same either way — the certifier charges their own professional fee on top.

    Many people find a private certifier is worth the fee — they can catch missing documents before lodgement and optimise the construction schedule to the certification process. As the applicant, it is your decision what will work best for you.

  • What documents do I need to lodge a building application for a new rural house?
    ⌄

    For a Class 1a dwelling (new house or extension), the full lodgement package is:

    • Completed DA Form 2 (the QLD Government building development application form)
    • Site Plan, Floor Plan, Elevations, and Structural Details
    • Footing / Slab Design with Form 15 signed by a Registered Professional Engineer of Queensland (RPEQ)
    • Soil Test Report
    • Energy Efficiency Report (houses must meet 6-Star)
    • Bushfire Attack Level (BAL) Report — if the site is in the Bushfire Hazard Overlay
    • QBCC Insurance Certificate if using a licensed builder and the work is $3,300 or more; or QBCC Owner Builder Permit if building yourself and the work is $11,000 or more
    • QLeave payment receipt if the build is valued at $150,000 or more (excl. GST)

    Council's Building Info Sheets list the full requirements. Incomplete applications are rejected, so it's worth checking the list twice before lodging.

    You cannot commence any building work until you have received the Decision Notice (Approval). Starting early — even clearing or pouring footings — creates unapproved work that has to be undone or retrospectively certified at your cost.
  • What if I'm building a shed first, then a house later?
    ⌄

    This is a common pathway on the Southern Downs — build a shed to store equipment in, then build the permanent house later when finances allow. Both stages still need proper approval, but the process is very similar for both.

    A shed (Class 10a — sheds, garages, carports) requires its own building application with essentially the same checklist of documents as a new dwelling:

    • Completed DA Form 2
    • Site Plan, Floor Plan, Elevations, and Structural Details
    • Footing / Slab Design with Form 15 signed by an RPEQ, and a soil investigation report (unless the footing design is generic and suits all soil types)
    • Form 15 for the steel frame (most rural sheds)
    • QBCC Insurance Certificate or Owner Builder Permit as above
    • QLeave receipt if the shed is valued at $150,000 or more (excl. GST)

    Key differences from a Class 1a house application: a shed doesn't need an energy efficiency report, and doesn't need the 6-Star rating. It still needs a BAL report if in the Bushfire Hazard Overlay and located within 6m of a residence.

    When you later apply for the house, it's a separate Class 1a application with the full document list. The shed doesn't "count" toward the house approval — they're assessed independently.

  • What about tiny homes, prefabricated houses, or shipping container builds?
    ⌄

    Short answer: a smaller or alternative building does not mean less approval. These structures get asked about constantly, and the most common misconception is that "tiny" or "prefab" means the normal rules don't apply. They do.

    Tiny homes — fixed to the land. Whether prefabricated or built on site, a tiny home permanently fixed to the land and used as a primary or secondary home is treated like any other Class 1a dwelling. Same building approval process, same document list, same inspections, same Form 21 at the end. Council removed the minimum 60m² floor area requirement in August 2024, so there's no "too small" threshold for a Class 1 dwelling in the Southern Downs.

    Tiny homes — on wheels. Treated like caravans. They can be stored on the land without approval if they're genuinely just being stored (not lived in, not connected to services). Living in one on your land is only permitted under a Temporary Home permit (see the question below about living on the land during construction) or if parked within an approved Caravan Park.

    Prefabricated and manufactured homes. Can be approved as Class 1a dwellings, but the paperwork is often harder — not easier. Many commercially sold tiny homes and modular units are imported and may not meet Australian standards. Before buying, ask the supplier for written certification that building, plumbing and electrical works meet Queensland requirements, including RPEQ signoff. Things a certifier will want to verify include structural adequacy, ceiling height, energy efficiency (7-Star rating), termite barriers, damp and weatherproofing, fire safety, Form 15 for structural elements, Form 43 for waterproofing, and the ability to get QBCC insurance. If these certifications can't be produced, a building approval may not be obtainable — and without one, a Form 21 can't be issued.

    Shipping containers. Depends entirely on the intended use:

    • Self-assessable (no approval): temporary short-term storage (under 6 months) on residential land during renovations or removals, provided it's not fixed to the ground, not on a road or road reserve, and doesn't affect community amenity. Also self-assessable for short-term storage on industrial, rural, or active construction sites.
    • Building approval required: any use as a building (house, secondary dwelling, bedroom, office, shed, workshop, hobby room); any use as a structure (swimming pool, fence, viewing platform, architectural feature); long-term storage (over 6 months); anchored to the ground with footings, slabs, electricity or plumbing; or altered with doors, windows, awnings, or attachments.
    • Amenity & Aesthetics application: required before building approval when the container is in any Urban or Rural Residential zone, or in the Rural zone if within 100m of a residential area, on a lot 4,000m² or less, or within 20m of a property boundary. Conditions commonly imposed include painting, concrete slab or 400mm stumps (to prevent rabbit harbourage — a Darling Downs Moreton Rabbit Board requirement), and sometimes a pitched roof.

    Council has dedicated fact sheets on both tiny houses and shipping containers. Given how specific the rules are to each situation, please call Council on 1300 697 372 before committing funds — a 10-minute conversation can save thousands.

  • What is a Form 15, Form 16, and Form 21?
    ⌄

    These are standard certificates used throughout the Queensland building process. You'll see them mentioned constantly:

    Form 15 — Compliance Certificate for Building Design or Specification. Signed by an RPEQ (registered engineer) to confirm that part of the design meets code. Commonly provided for footings, slabs, and steel framing. Required before building approval can be issued.
    Form 16 — Inspection Certificate / Aspect Certificate. Signed by the building certifier (or another competent person) after each stage of construction to confirm that stage passes. You'll collect four of these over the build — footing, slab, frame, final.
    Form 21 — Final Inspection Certificate. Issued by the certifier once the final inspection passes and all conditions of approval are met. It's the document that says the building is complete and lawful. For new dwellings, you cannot move in until Form 21 is issued.

    Your certifier manages all of these. Keep copies — they're important when you sell the house or change insurance.

  • What inspections happen during construction?
    ⌄

    For a Class 1a dwelling, the certifier must inspect and sign off at four stages. Each stage must pass before the next commences:

    1. Footing / Foundation — before concrete is poured. Confirms excavation depth, reinforcement, and placement match the engineer's design.
    2. Slab — after slab prep, before the pour. Confirms reinforcement, plumbing penetrations, and setout.
    3. Frame — once framing (walls, roof structure) is up and before sheeting goes on. Confirms tie-down, bracing, and member sizes. This is often where problems are caught.
    4. Final — at completion. Everything: stairs, balustrades, smoke alarms, waterproofing, energy efficiency, safety, and all conditions of approval.

    Your builder arranges inspections directly with your certifier. Plumbing has its own separate inspection schedule managed by Council plumbing inspectors — your plumber handles those bookings.

    If a stage fails, the certifier issues a written notice of what needs to be rectified. The work must be corrected and re-inspected before construction continues. If a licensed builder refuses to rectify defective work, the QBCC can issue a formal Direction to Rectify — see the QBCC.

  • When can I actually move in?
    ⌄

    For a new dwelling, you can move in once the Form 21 Final Certificate has been issued. Not before — even if the house feels finished.

    The Form 21 is only issued after the final inspection passes and all conditions of approval are satisfied. Common last-minute holdups: smoke alarm interconnection, balustrade compliance on decks over 1m, final stormwater discharge, and landscaping required by approval conditions.

    Occupying a new dwelling before Form 21 is an offence under the Building Act 1975. It can also affect your insurance.
  • Can I live on the land in a caravan or shed while I build?
    ⌄

    On rural land, yes — but only with Council approval, and only after you've received building approval for the permanent dwelling. It is not automatic, and it is not allowed in urban residential areas.

    The process:

    1. Obtain building approval for the dwelling first.
    2. Complete an Application for Permission to Occupy Temporary Accommodation and submit it to Council at least 30 days before your intended start date.
    3. Pay the applicable fee.
    4. Supply supporting information: drawings of the proposed temporary home, wastewater disposal plan, drinking water supply (minimum 5,000L tank required), landowner consent if you're not the owner, and a clear reason for needing temporary occupation.

    Approval can be issued for up to 18 months, and Council may extend it to match the expected completion of the permanent dwelling. The temporary accommodation approval lapses automatically once the dwelling is finished, the building approval lapses, or the approval term ends.

    Tents and camper trailers are not considered suitable temporary homes. A shed (Class 10a with prior building approval), caravan, or mobile home is. Full detail: SDRC — Temporary Homes.

    Don't start living on site before approval is granted. It can void the temporary approval entirely and create enforcement issues.
  • How do I arrange rubbish collection once I've moved in?
    ⌄

    Kerbside collection in the Southern Downs is contracted to Cleanaway. To start a residential service on a new dwelling, Cleanaway requires a Form 21 Final Certificate as proof the dwelling is complete and lawfully occupied. Until Form 21 is issued, a new property can't be added to the collection round.

    The garbage truck doesn't go everywhere. Many rural properties fall outside the Designated Waste Collection Area, so kerbside pickup isn't available. In those areas, Council operates rural bin compounds — shared, gated facilities where ratepayers can drop off their own waste. Residents living in a compound catchment can apply to Council for a key. Call 1300 697 372 to check whether your property is in a compound catchment and to request a key.

    Private collection. If your property is outside both kerbside coverage and a bin compound catchment, several private waste operators service rural properties in the region. Ask around locally, or call Council for a starting point.

  • Where do I get water if my tanks run low?
    ⌄

    Many bulk water carting businesses operate across the Southern Downs region and can deliver to rural tanks. Council maintains a list of approved water carters on the Approved Water Carters page.

    If you're arranging a delivery and want to confirm that a carter is licensed and approved to draw from Council's filling stations, call SDRC on 1300 697 372 and staff can confirm approval status.

    Planning your water storage from the start saves money on carting over time. Rural dwellings are required to have on-site storage of 45,000L for 1–2 bedrooms, 67,500L for more — these minimums exist to get you through a normal dry spell without needing deliveries.
  • What costs and fees should I budget for beyond construction?
    ⌄

    Beyond the construction itself, these are the items to keep in mind when budgeting for a new rural home. For current dollar figures, request quotes from the relevant providers and check the current SDRC Fees & Charges schedule.

    • Council building application fee — refer to the current SDRC Fees & Charges.
    • Private certifier fee, if using one.
    • Engineer (RPEQ) — for footing/slab design and Form 15, plus soil test.
    • Energy efficiency assessment — required for all new dwellings.
    • BAL assessment — only if in the Bushfire Hazard Overlay.
    • QBCC Home Warranty Insurance — required for residential work over $3,300. Paid by the builder and added to the contract price.
    • QLeave levy — required for work over $150,000 (excl. GST). Must be paid before building approval is issued.
    • Planning application fee — only if your site needs an MCU. Separate from building fees.
    • Pre-lodgement meeting — optional, $300 for 60 minutes with Council planners for tailored advice.
    • Infrastructure costs — rural sites often need driveway construction, stormwater management, on-site water storage (45,000L for 1–2 bedrooms, 67,500L for more), and on-site sewage treatment. Get quotes early.
  • I'm overwhelmed. Where do I start?
    ⌄

    Totally normal feeling. Here's the shortest sensible first week:

    1. Open the Online Mapping tool and confirm your zone and overlays.
    2. Print and work through the Rural Zone Checklist. Mark each item ✓ / ✗ / NA.
    3. Call Council on 1300 697 372 and ask to speak to the duty planner (general enquiries are free). Have your address, your ticks, and any ✗ items ready.
    4. Based on that call, get 2–3 quotes from licensed builders and, if going that route, from a private certifier.
    5. If any item is ✗, consider booking a Pre-Lodgement Meeting before investing further.

    At every step, Council staff are happy to help you understand the process — it's not a test, and you don't have to know the terminology before you ring. That's what the duty planner is there for.

  • My neighbour's dog barks constantly. Can Council do something?
    ⌄

    Yes. Persistent barking is a noise nuisance Council can investigate under local laws. The process works in steps:

    1. Try direct contact first. In many cases the owner isn't aware — a friendly note often resolves it without Council involvement.
    2. Lodge a service request via Council's online service request form or by calling 1300 697 372.
    3. Keep a noise diary. Council will ask you to record dates, times, and how the barking affects you. This is essential — without it, Council has limited ability to take action.
    4. Council investigates. Once the diary is received, a Local Laws Officer will contact the dog owner.
    Download the Barking Dogs Fact Sheet and Noise Diary to get started.
  • Why does Council need a noise diary? Why can't an officer just come and listen?
    ⌄

    A noise diary creates an objective record showing a pattern — which Local Laws Officers need to demonstrate a genuine nuisance under the legislation. It also protects you by documenting the problem factually.

  • I've found a stray dog. What should I do?
    ⌄

  • I've been attacked by a dog. What do I do?
    ⌄
    If anyone needs emergency medical attention, call 000 first.

    Once you're safe, call Council on 1300 697 372. A Local Laws Officer will attend the scene. You will also need to submit a Statement of Dog Attack form — email the completed form to mail@sdrc.qld.gov.au. When you call, have ready: the location, a description of the dog, whether it is still in the area, and the time of the attack.

  • I've been told I have a water leak. How do I find it?
    ⌄

    Many leaks can be found without a plumber. Check inside first — a running toilet is the most common cause. Add a few drops of food colouring to the cistern and wait 10 minutes without flushing; if colour appears in the bowl, the cistern is leaking. Also check all taps, showerheads, and washing machine connections.

    Outside, look for unusually green or boggy ground — this can indicate an underground pipe failure even when there's no visible surface water. Check garden timers and hose guns too.

    The Checking for Water Leaks Fact Sheet includes a method for testing underground pipes without a plumber.
  • There's a leak at the meter or on the footpath — is that Council's responsibility?
    ⌄

    Council's side (the meter itself, or the pipe between the water main and the meter): report to Council immediately on 1300 697 372.

    Your side (the pipe running from the meter into the property): this is the property owner's responsibility. Consider shutting off the water at the meter while you arrange a licensed plumber.

    Not sure which side? Call 1300 697 372 and we can help work it out.

  • Can I get a rates adjustment if I had an unexpected leak?
    ⌄

    In some circumstances Council may consider an adjustment for water lost to an undetected leak. Contact Council's Water Team on 1300 697 372 to discuss. Having your repair invoice available will assist with the assessment.

  • How can I monitor my water usage between bills?
    ⌄

    The My SDRC Water portal connects to your Smart Water Meter and lets you view near-real-time usage and set a custom alert threshold to catch a leak early. Sign-up details are on page 4 of your rates notice. See also: Smart Water Meters.

  • Where is my water meter?
    ⌄

    Water meters are usually near the front boundary of the property close to the street — often in a small covered box set into the ground. If you can't find yours, call 1300 697 372 and we can look up the exact location for your address.

  • Where do I take this? — Recycle Mate does the thinking for you
    ⌄

    Recycle Mate is a free app (and web tool) that tells you exactly how and where to dispose of almost anything. Snap a photo or type the item name, and it returns locations specific to the Southern Downs — including Council waste facilities, supermarkets running soft plastic or battery collection, pharmacies taking unused medication, and charity shops accepting reusable items.

    It's the fastest way to answer "where does this go?" without having to call Council. Download from the app store, or use the SDRC Recycle Mate page.

    If Recycle Mate doesn't cover your item, or you'd like to double-check, call Council on 1300 697 372 and the waste team can advise.
  • My bin wasn't collected. What do I do?
    ⌄

    Lodge a request via Council's online service request form or call 1300 697 372. Cleanaway will schedule a return collection before your next regular collection day.

    Before you lodge: confirm the bin was at the kerbside by 6:00am and wasn't blocked by a parked vehicle — these are the most common reasons a bin is passed.
  • What day is my bin collected?
    ⌄

    Your collection day is tied to your property address. Search your address using SDRC's online mapping tool to find your bin day, or call 1300 697 372. Collection days may change on public holidays — Council will publish any changes in advance.

  • What goes in which bin?
    ⌄

    Red or Green lid (general waste, weekly) — kitchen and food waste, general household rubbish, plastic bags and cling wrap, nappies, and polystyrene foam.

    Yellow lid (recycling, fortnightly) — paper and cardboard, steel and aluminium cans, rigid plastic bottles and containers (lids removed), glass bottles and jars (lids removed).

    Not sure about a specific item? Use Recycle Mate (first question above) — it's built exactly for this.

    Never put batteries in either bin — they can cause serious truck fires. See the batteries question below.

    Full detail: What goes in my bins — SDRC.

  • My bin is damaged or missing. Can I get a replacement?
    ⌄

    Yes. Lodge a request via Council's online service request form or call 1300 697 372. Include your address and describe the issue. Damaged bins are repaired where possible; if a bin is not serviceable or has been stolen, it will be replaced.

  • I'm elderly or have a disability and can't put my bins out. Is there help available?
    ⌄

    Yes. Council offers an Infirm Collection Service for residents unable to put bins out due to age, disability, or medical immobility. Cleanaway drivers collect the bins from the property and return them once emptied, as part of their normal round.

    The service is only available if nobody else at the property is able to present the bins. To apply, email mail@sdrc.qld.gov.au with a letter requesting the service and a confirmation letter or medical certificate from a registered health practitioner (subject line: ATTN Waste Team).

  • I don't have a bin service — can I get one?
    ⌄

    Depends where the property sits in the Designated Waste Collection Area. There are three categories:

    • Compulsory — service is mandatory and the charge appears on your rates notice.
    • Opt-In / Opt-Out — you may choose whether to take the service.
    • Voluntary — you can request a service, and Cleanaway decides whether the property can be serviced based on route and access.

    To request a new service, email mail@sdrc.qld.gov.au with a letter requesting the service and accepting that charges will be added to your rates notice (subject line: ATTN Waste Team). If approved, bins are delivered to the property.

    Properties outside the collection area entirely can access one of Council's waste facilities directly, or use a rural bin compound if one services your area. These are locked, unsupervised bin compounds — access is by key, issued only to approved residents within the catchment area. Call 1300 697 372 to check whether your property falls within a compound catchment.

  • How do I dispose of batteries, phones, and e-waste?
    ⌄
    Never put batteries in a kerbside bin. They can short-circuit, catch fire, and damage trucks or facilities. This is one of the fastest-growing causes of waste fires in Australia.

    Batteries under 5kg — Council has partnered with Ecobatt, and dedicated battery collection containers are available at all supervised Council waste facilities. Best practice is to tape the terminals (particularly on lithium batteries) and hand the battery to a facility attendant rather than dropping it in. Accepted types include:

    • Household alkaline — AA, AAA, C, D, PP3, button cells
    • Lithium / rechargeable — mobile phones, laptops, tablets, cameras, e-cigarettes and vapes, portable tools, e-scooters and bikes, power banks
    • Lead acid — car, vehicle, caravan batteries (ensure caps are tight; speak to an attendant if the casing is damaged)

    Recycle Mate also lists private drop-off points — many pharmacies, supermarkets, and electronics retailers run their own battery and phone collection programs, which may be closer than a waste facility.

    E-waste (computers, TVs, accessories) is accepted at all supervised Council waste facilities for recycling. Damaged batteries — speak to a facility attendant before attending.

  • How do I dispose of green waste, garden clippings, and trickle tape?
    ⌄

    Green waste — grass clippings, prunings, small branches, and similar — can go in your red or green lid general waste bin. Do not put green waste in the yellow recycling bin.

    Larger loads can be taken to a Council waste facility. Green waste disposal is free for residents; commercial green waste attracts a disposal fee. Recycling at home through composting or mulching is also a great option — see Council's Worm Farming and Composting fact sheets.

    Trickle tape (T-tape) — Council stockpiles clean trickle tape for recycling at the Stanthorpe Waste Management Facility, free of charge. The tape must be separated from plastic mulch and as free as possible from soil, roots, and organic matter. Tape that's contaminated cannot be recycled and attracts landfill fees for commercial operators. Site supervisors will direct you to the stockpile on arrival.

    Please don't dump green waste on bushland, verges, or waterways — it spreads pest plants, fuels bushfires, and can attract wildlife to roadsides. See "Reporting illegal dumping" below.

  • Where do I take items I can't put in the bin — mattresses, whitegoods, chemicals, asbestos?
    ⌄

    Start with Recycle Mate — it will often find a closer or cheaper option than a Council facility. For most other items, Council's waste facilities accept them directly. Locations, opening hours, and accepted materials are on the Waste Facilities page.

    Free at Council facilities: scrap metal, clean concrete and bricks, e-waste, empty agvet chemical containers (drumMUSTER), used motor oil at some sites, and clean timber pallets.

    Fees apply to: general household waste, mattresses, tyres, whitegoods, construction and demolition waste. Fees are set in Council's annual Fees & Charges schedule.

    Asbestos — pre-booking required. Asbestos must be double-bagged in 200-micron plastic, clearly labelled, and pre-booked with Council before attending the facility. Facility staff cannot accept asbestos without a booking. Call 1300 697 372 to arrange.

    Deceased pets — small pets (birds, fish, guinea pigs) can go in the general waste bin. Larger pets should be taken to the closest accepting waste facility; burial is only available at the Warwick Waste Management Facility. Prior notice is required for bulk loads.

    Ammunition, explosives, and old fireworks — never go in any bin or waste facility. Contact the Queensland Explosives Inspectorate on 1300 739 868 (24-hour hotline).

  • Someone has dumped rubbish on my land or in a public place. How do I report it?
    ⌄

    Illegal dumping is any unlawful deposit of 200 litres or more of waste — roughly the volume of a wheelie bin — in bushland, roadsides, waterways, outside charity bins, on the kerbside outside of collection time, or at the wrong location within a waste facility.

    Lodge a service request via Council's online service request form or by calling 1300 697 372. You can pin the exact location on the map and attach photos directly through the form.

    Include as much detail as you can:

    • Time, date, place, and description of the waste
    • Vehicle registration, make, model, colour, or distinguishing features
    • Description of the person (location in vehicle, clothing, gender)
    Don't approach the person depositing the waste. Take photos only if safe to do so.

    Fines are significant — individuals and corporations can face substantial on-the-spot penalties, with much higher amounts available if the matter goes to court. Current figures are in the Illegal Dumping fact sheet.

    Worth knowing: almost all domestic waste can be disposed of at Council facilities for free. The cost of doing it properly is much less than the fine for dumping.

  • How do I report a pothole or road damage?
    ⌄

    Report online using Council's online service request form — attach a screenshot with a map pin showing the exact location. Alternatively call 1300 697 372. Include the street name, nearest landmark, and an estimate of the size.

  • Is the damaged road Council's or the State Government's responsibility?
    ⌄

    Not all roads in the region are Council's responsibility. Major highways are managed by the Department of Transport and Main Roads (TMR).

    • State roads (New England Hwy, Cunningham Hwy, other TMR roads): contact TMR — 13 20 80
    • Rail crossings / railway tracks: contact Queensland Rail — 13 16 17
    • Council roads: lodge a request with Council as above

    Not sure? Call Council on 1300 697 372 and we can help.

  • What disputes can Council help with?
    ⌄

    Council's Local Laws team can investigate: persistent noise nuisances (barking dogs, loud machinery, amplified music); dust, smoke, odour, or light pollution; water pollution from a neighbouring property; unauthorised building works or illegal use of a structure; and unlawful land use.

    Lodge a complaint via Council's online service request form or call 1300 697 372.

  • What disputes are outside Council's jurisdiction?
    ⌄

    Some common disputes are private matters Council cannot mediate or enforce — including boundary and fencing disputes, trees crossing property lines, parking outside the Warwick CBD, and easement disputes. For these matters the Queensland Civil and Administrative Tribunal (QCAT) is often the right avenue: qcat.qld.gov.au or 1300 753 228.

  • There are weeds on a Council road near my property. Who do I contact?
    ⌄

    Council has a dedicated Invasive Pests program with officers responsible for weed control on Council roads and land. Report via Council's online service request form or call 1300 697 372.

    Weeds on the New England Highway or Cunningham Highway? These are State Government roads managed by TMR — contact TMR on 13 20 80.
